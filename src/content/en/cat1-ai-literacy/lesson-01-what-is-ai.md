## Why Start with This Question?

Before we explore how AI can help in teaching, we need to establish a clear understanding of what AI actually is — and what it is not. Misconceptions about AI are everywhere: in the media, in staffroom conversations, and in the way technology companies market their products. Some of these misconceptions lead to unnecessary fear, others to unrealistic expectations. Having a solid, honest foundation will make everything else in this course easier to understand and apply.

This lesson is deliberately non-technical. You do not need a background in computer science to understand what AI is. In fact, you already have many of the skills needed to work with it effectively — but more on that at the end.

## What AI Is — and What It Is Not

Artificial intelligence is a field of computer science focused on developing systems that can perform tasks typically associated with human cognitive abilities: recognizing patterns, understanding language, making decisions, and generating content.

However, the term "intelligence" can be deeply misleading. When we hear the word, we naturally think of human intelligence — the kind that involves understanding, feeling, reasoning about abstract ideas, and being aware of what we are doing. AI has none of these qualities.

AI systems process information through mathematical operations on large amounts of data. They can produce outputs that *look* intelligent — a well-written paragraph, a correct translation, a realistic image — but the process behind those outputs is fundamentally different from human thought. There is no understanding, no consciousness, no intention.

> **Key Insight**: AI does not think like humans. It processes information through statistical patterns learned from enormous amounts of data. The outputs can be remarkably useful, but the process is mathematical, not cognitive.

### What AI Can Do

AI systems today are capable of impressive feats within specific domains. They can recognize patterns in text, images, and data with remarkable accuracy. They can generate human-like text in dozens of languages, create images from written descriptions, translate between languages in real time, summarize long documents into concise overviews, and answer questions based on patterns learned during training.

These capabilities are genuinely useful. A teacher who understands them can save significant time on routine tasks like creating worksheets, adapting materials for different reading levels, or brainstorming ideas for lessons.

### What AI Cannot Do

Equally important is understanding what AI *cannot* do, no matter how impressive it sometimes appears. AI cannot truly understand meaning or context the way humans do — it processes statistical relationships between words, not ideas. It cannot feel emotions or have genuine opinions, even when it generates text that sounds opinionated. It cannot guarantee accuracy — AI regularly produces confident-sounding but incorrect information, a phenomenon known as *hallucination*. And it cannot replace human judgment, creativity, empathy, or the ability to truly know your students.

This last point deserves emphasis. The things that make a great teacher — reading a room, noticing when a student is struggling, adapting a lesson on the fly based on intuition built over years of experience — are precisely the things AI cannot do.

## Common Myths About AI

Before going further, it is worth addressing three myths that come up frequently when teachers first encounter AI.

**Myth 1: "AI knows everything."** AI models are trained on data up to a certain date. They do not have access to real-time information unless specifically connected to the internet through additional tools. More importantly, they do not *know* anything in the way you know things. They have learned statistical patterns that allow them to generate plausible text. This means they can generate incorrect information with complete confidence — presenting a fabricated citation or a wrong date as though it were established fact.

**Myth 2: "AI is going to replace teachers."** AI is a tool. Like calculators, the internet, and interactive whiteboards before it, AI changes how we work rather than simply replacing us. History shows that technologies that were predicted to replace teachers — from television in the 1950s to MOOCs in the 2010s — never did. What actually changes is how teachers use their time and what skills they need. Teachers who understand AI will be better equipped to prepare students for a world where these tools are commonplace.

**Myth 3: "AI understands what I'm saying."** AI processes patterns in language. When you type a question and receive a remarkably relevant response, it is easy to feel that you are being understood. In reality, the AI has learned from billions of text examples what kind of response typically follows your kind of question. This is statistical pattern matching, not comprehension — a subtle but crucial distinction that affects how you should evaluate and use AI outputs.

## What About Chatbots?

When most people think of AI today, they think of chatbots like ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini. It is important to understand that a chatbot is a *user interface* — a way for humans to interact with an AI system through conversation. The chatbot itself is not "the AI."

Behind the chatbot are large language models (LLMs), which are the actual AI systems that process and generate text. Think of it this way: a chatbot is like a phone — it is the device you use to communicate. The LLM is the system on the other end that processes your input and generates a response.

This distinction matters because AI is not limited to chat interfaces. The same underlying technology powers translation tools, image generators, code assistants, and many other applications. Understanding that chatbots are just one way to access AI helps you see the broader landscape of tools available to you.

## Narrow AI vs. General AI

The AI we use today is **narrow AI** (also called weak AI) — it is designed for specific tasks. ChatGPT is very good at generating text, but it cannot drive a car. An image recognition system can identify objects in photos, but it cannot write poetry. Each AI tool excels within a defined scope and fails outside it.

**Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)** — a hypothetical AI that could perform any intellectual task a human can do, with the same flexibility and adaptability — does not yet exist. All current AI tools are narrow AI, no matter how impressive they seem. We explore AGI further in a later lesson, but for now, the key point is simple: the AI you encounter today is a powerful specialist, not a general-purpose thinker.

## AI Is Already Part of Education

You may already be encountering AI in education without realizing it. Here are some common examples:

| Application | Example | AI Type |
|---|---|---|
| Adaptive learning | Platforms that adjust difficulty based on student performance (Khan Academy, Duolingo) | Machine learning |
| Automated grading | Systems that grade multiple-choice tests and provide basic feedback | Pattern recognition |
| Language translation | Google Translate, DeepL | Neural machine translation |
| Writing assistance | Grammar checkers like Grammarly | Natural language processing |
| Content generation | ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini | Large language models |
| Recommendation | YouTube suggesting educational videos based on viewing history | Recommendation algorithms |

Many of these tools have been part of education for years. What has changed recently is the arrival of large language models that can interact through natural conversation — making AI feel much more present and personal than a spell-checker ever did.

## Why This Matters for Teachers

Here is perhaps the most encouraging message in this entire course: teachers already possess many competencies that transfer directly to working with AI.

**Defining clear goals** — just like writing a lesson plan, working with AI requires clarity about what you want to achieve. A vague request produces a vague result, whether you are giving it to a student or to an AI.

**Structuring tasks** — breaking down complex assignments into manageable steps is exactly how you get the best results from AI. Teachers do this every day when they scaffold learning for students.

**Evaluating outputs** — teachers are experts at assessing quality. Is this explanation accurate? Is it appropriate for the age group? Is it well-structured? These are the same questions you should ask when reviewing AI-generated content.

**Adapting based on results** — when a lesson does not work as planned, you adjust. When an AI output is not quite right, you refine your request and try again. This iterative process is second nature to experienced teachers.

Understanding what AI is — clearly and honestly — gives you the foundation to explore it with confidence, use it with judgment, and help your students develop a healthy, informed relationship with these tools.

## Summary

- Artificial intelligence is a field of computer science that builds systems capable of performing tasks associated with human cognition — but AI does not think, feel, or understand
- AI processes statistical patterns learned from data, which explains both its impressive capabilities and its tendency to generate confident-sounding errors
- Common myths — that AI knows everything, will replace teachers, or understands what you say — are all incorrect, and understanding why helps you use AI more effectively
- Chatbots are user interfaces; the actual AI is the large language model behind them
- All current AI is *narrow AI* — powerful within specific tasks, but unable to generalize like humans
- Teachers already possess core competencies (goal-setting, task structuring, output evaluation, iteration) that transfer directly to effective AI use
