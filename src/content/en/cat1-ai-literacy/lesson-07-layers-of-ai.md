## Why Think in Layers?

One of the most common misunderstandings about AI is the perception that it is a single, unified tool — usually a chatbot you type messages to. When teachers first encounter AI through ChatGPT or Claude, it is natural to assume that what you see in that conversation window is everything AI can do.

In reality, the ways we can use AI have developed gradually, and today they can be understood as *layers*, where each layer extends the capabilities of the one before it. This is not an official technical classification — it is a didactic framework designed to give you a clear mental map of what AI can do, what it cannot do, and where the tools you use fit into the bigger picture.

Understanding these layers helps you set realistic expectations, choose the right tool for the right task, and anticipate how AI capabilities will evolve in the coming years.

## Layer 1: Basic Language Models and Chatbots

The first and most widespread layer consists of basic language models accessible through chatbot interfaces. You ask a question, and the model generates a text response. The interaction is linear and happens exclusively through language.

**What this looks like in practice:** You open ChatGPT or Claude, type a question like "Explain photosynthesis for a 10-year-old," and receive a text answer.

**Educational uses at this layer:**

- Explaining concepts in simpler language
- Generating examples and practice exercises
- Summarizing long texts
- Creating worksheets and lesson plan drafts
- Brainstorming ideas for projects or activities

Although this layer is often underestimated, it already represents a powerful tool in a teacher's hands. Most teachers currently operate at this level, and there is enormous value to be extracted from it before moving to higher layers.

> **Key Insight**: You do not need to rush to the most advanced layers. Layer 1 alone — simple text-based interaction with a chatbot — can save hours of preparation time and improve the quality of your materials if used well.

## Layer 2: Multimodal Models

At the second layer, AI is no longer limited to text. Models can simultaneously process images, audio, and video — they begin to *see* and *hear*.

**What this looks like in practice:** You upload a photograph of a student's handwritten essay to Claude and ask for feedback. You share a chart with ChatGPT and ask it to explain the trends. You record audio and have AI transcribe and translate it.

**Educational uses at this layer:**

- Analyzing photographs, diagrams, charts, and student work
- Generating images for teaching materials
- Converting text to speech for accessibility
- Transcribing and summarizing audio recordings
- Creating visual explanations of abstract concepts

This layer marks the transition from purely text-based interaction to working with real, multimedia content — which is much closer to how teaching actually works.

## Layer 3: Models That Use Tools

The third layer emerges when models gain the ability to *use tools*. AI no longer just generates a response — it can search the internet, analyze documents, work with data, perform calculations, or execute more complex tasks.

**What this looks like in practice:** You ask Claude to search for the latest research on a topic and summarize what it finds. You ask ChatGPT to analyze a spreadsheet of student test scores and identify patterns. The AI accesses external information rather than relying solely on what it learned during training.

**Educational uses at this layer:**

- Finding and summarizing current information (not limited to training data)
- Comparing multiple sources on a topic
- Analyzing data sets (test results, survey data, attendance records)
- Running calculations and creating visualizations
- Preparing more comprehensive, research-backed materials

> **Key Insight**: The difference between Layer 2 and Layer 3 is the difference between an AI that *knows things* and an AI that can *find things out*. Layer 3 models can access current information, making them far more useful for real-world tasks.

## Layer 4: AI That Uses Computers

The next step in development is the ability for AI to use a computer in a way similar to a human. This includes opening applications, managing files, clicking buttons, filling forms, and performing actions within a digital environment.

**What this looks like in practice:** You ask AI to open a specific website, navigate to a particular page, extract information, and organize it into a document. Or you ask it to format a spreadsheet, create a presentation, or manage files across different folders.

**How this differs from Layer 3:** In Layer 3, AI gets access to individual tools and functions. In Layer 4, it gets access to an entire working environment — your computer screen, your applications, your file system. The scope of what it can do expands dramatically.

**Educational uses at this layer:**

- Automating administrative tasks (filling forms, organizing files)
- Creating materials across multiple applications in sequence
- Testing student-facing digital tools and websites
- Setting up digital learning environments

This layer is still emerging, and most teachers will not interact with it directly yet. But it is important to know it exists because it represents the direction in which AI tools are moving.

## Layer 5: Agents

Agents represent a major shift in how AI works. Instead of waiting for each new instruction, an agent is given a *goal*, and it independently plans and executes the steps needed to achieve it.

**What this looks like in practice:** Instead of giving AI step-by-step instructions ("First find articles about climate change, then summarize the three most recent ones, then create a worksheet based on them"), you simply say: "Prepare a reading comprehension activity about recent climate change developments for my 8th-grade class." The agent figures out the steps on its own.

**How this differs from earlier layers:** In Layers 1-4, you are the driver — you give instructions, AI executes them, you give more instructions. With agents, you define the destination, and the AI plans the route.

**Educational uses at this layer:**

- Comprehensive lesson preparation from a single goal description
- Gathering materials, adapting them to a specific audience, and suggesting activities — all autonomously
- Creating differentiated materials for multiple student groups simultaneously
- Research and synthesis tasks that would otherwise require many individual prompts

> **Warning**: The teacher's role becomes even more important with agents. Because agents work more autonomously, the quality of your initial goal description and your review of the final output matter enormously. Agents are powerful, but they still need human oversight.

## Layer 6: Agent Systems

In the most advanced layer, multiple agents collaborate as a team. Each agent has a specific role — one plans, another gathers information, a third creates content, and a fourth checks quality.

**What this looks like in practice:** Imagine asking an AI system to prepare an entire unit of study. One agent researches the topic, another designs the lesson sequence, a third creates student materials, a fourth builds an assessment, and a fifth reviews everything for accuracy and alignment with curriculum standards.

**Educational uses at this layer:**

- End-to-end curriculum development
- Comprehensive school or department-level planning
- Creation of complete course materials including lessons, assessments, and resources
- Automated quality assurance and alignment checking

This layer represents the direction in which AI is currently developing. While full agent systems are not yet part of everyday teaching practice, understanding them helps you see where the technology is heading and how the teacher's role as director and decision-maker becomes even more central — not less.

## Putting It All Together: The Layer Map

| Layer | What AI Does | Teacher's Role | Available Now? |
|-------|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| 1. Basic chatbot | Generates text responses | Asks questions, evaluates answers | Yes — widely |
| 2. Multimodal | Processes text, images, audio, video | Provides materials, reviews outputs | Yes — widely |
| 3. Tool-using | Searches, analyzes data, uses external tools | Defines tasks, verifies information | Yes — most major tools |
| 4. Computer-using | Operates applications and digital environments | Oversees processes, checks results | Emerging |
| 5. Agents | Plans and executes multi-step tasks independently | Sets goals, reviews final outputs | Emerging |
| 6. Agent systems | Multiple AI agents collaborate on complex tasks | Directs strategy, makes final decisions | Early development |

## Where Do You Fit?

Most teachers today are working at Layers 1-2, and that is perfectly fine. There is an enormous amount of value to be extracted from simply having good conversations with a chatbot and uploading images for analysis.

The point of understanding layers is not to push yourself to the most advanced level as quickly as possible. It is to:

- **Know what is possible** — so you can choose the right approach for each task
- **Set realistic expectations** — a Layer 1 tool cannot do Layer 5 tasks
- **Avoid overestimating or underestimating AI** — each layer has clear strengths and clear limits
- **Plan your own learning** — you can deliberately move through layers at your own pace

## Summary

- AI capabilities can be understood through six layers, from basic chatbots to multi-agent systems
- Each layer extends the capabilities of the previous one, giving AI more autonomy and broader skills
- Most teachers currently work at Layers 1-2 (text conversations and image analysis), and there is great value at this level
- Layers 3-4 (tool use and computer use) are becoming available in major AI platforms
- Layers 5-6 (agents and agent systems) represent the near future of AI
- The teacher's role becomes *more* important as AI becomes more capable — providing goals, context, oversight, and judgment remains irreplaceable
- Use this framework as a mental map to understand where you are, where the tools are heading, and what to expect next
