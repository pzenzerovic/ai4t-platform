## Why Understanding Limitations Matters

Using AI effectively requires knowing not just what it can do, but where it falls short. Teachers who understand AI limitations can set appropriate expectations and model critical evaluation for students.

## Hallucinations: When AI Gets It Wrong

**Hallucination** is the term for when an AI model generates information that sounds confident and plausible but is actually incorrect. This happens because:

- The model predicts likely text, not verified facts
- It may combine patterns from different contexts incorrectly
- It has no mechanism to verify its own outputs against reality

### Examples in Education

- An AI might cite a research paper that doesn't exist
- It could attribute a quote to the wrong person
- It might provide outdated information as current fact
- Mathematical calculations can contain errors

> **Key Rule**: Always verify AI-generated content before using it in your classroom.

## What AI Is Good At

- **Generating first drafts** of text, lesson plans, and worksheets
- **Brainstorming** ideas and alternatives
- **Reformatting** content (e.g., turning notes into a structured outline)
- **Explaining concepts** in different ways or at different levels
- **Translation** and language adaptation

## What AI Struggles With

- **Factual accuracy** — especially for specific dates, statistics, and citations
- **Mathematical reasoning** — complex calculations can go wrong
- **Current events** — the model's knowledge has a training cutoff date
- **Nuanced judgment** — ethical decisions, cultural sensitivity, pedagogical choices
- **Consistency** — giving the same answer to the same question isn't guaranteed

## Implications for the Classroom

### For Teachers

1. **Always review** AI-generated materials before sharing with students
2. **Cross-reference** facts, dates, and statistics
3. **Use AI as a starting point**, not a final product
4. **Model critical thinking** by showing students how to evaluate AI output

### For Students

Teaching students about AI limitations is itself a valuable learning opportunity. Students who understand that AI can be wrong develop better critical thinking skills.

## The Context Problem

LLMs work within a **context window** — a limited amount of text they can consider at once. This means:

- Very long conversations may lose early context
- The model might "forget" instructions given earlier
- Complex, multi-step tasks can lead to inconsistencies

Understanding context helps you structure your interactions with AI more effectively.
