## AI Is Already in Your School

Even if you have not intentionally started using AI tools, chances are that AI is already part of your school environment. The spell-checker in your word processor uses AI. The platform that recommends educational videos to your students uses AI. The translation tool you rely on for multilingual communication uses AI. The adaptive learning app that adjusts difficulty for each student uses AI.

What has changed in the past few years is not that AI arrived in education — it was already there. What changed is that AI became *visible*. With the release of tools like ChatGPT and Claude, AI moved from being an invisible engine behind familiar tools to being a conversational partner that teachers and students interact with directly.

This lesson explores where AI already exists in education, what is genuinely new, and why your existing teaching skills are more relevant to this transition than you might expect.

## Adaptive Learning: AI That Adjusts to Each Student

One of the most established uses of AI in education is **adaptive learning** — platforms that continuously adjust what a student sees based on how they perform. These systems use machine learning algorithms to build a model of each student's knowledge and then select the most appropriate next activity.

If a student gets several math problems right in a row, the system increases the difficulty. If they struggle, it provides additional practice at the current level or offers a different explanation. Over time, the system builds a detailed profile of each student's strengths and weaknesses and uses it to personalize the learning path.

Examples include Khan Academy, Duolingo, and many national educational platforms. You may have used these tools without thinking of them as "AI" — but that is exactly what they are.

> **Key Insight**: Adaptive learning platforms are the most mature application of AI in education. They have been in use for years, and the evidence shows they can be effective when combined with good teaching — but they work best as supplements, not replacements, for teacher-led instruction.

## Automated Assessment: Helpful but Limited

AI-powered assessment tools have become increasingly sophisticated. They can grade multiple-choice and fill-in-the-blank tests instantly, provide basic feedback on writing (grammar, spelling, and structural suggestions), flag potential plagiarism, detect patterns in student performance across a class, and generate performance reports that highlight students who may need additional support.

These capabilities save time on routine assessment tasks and can provide teachers with useful data about class-wide patterns. However, it is essential to understand their limitations.

Automated grading cannot assess creativity, originality, or the nuanced reasoning that makes a student's argument compelling rather than merely correct. It cannot evaluate the quality of a student's thinking — only whether their output matches expected patterns. It cannot replace the professional judgment of a teacher who knows their students, understands the curriculum, and can recognize growth that does not show up in a score.

> **Warning**: Automated assessment is a time-saving tool for routine tasks, not a substitute for professional evaluation. Use it to handle the mechanical parts of assessment, and invest the time you save in the kinds of feedback only a human teacher can provide.

## Content Creation: The Biggest Recent Shift

The area where AI has changed most dramatically for teachers is **content creation**. With the arrival of large language models, teachers can now use AI to generate first drafts of lesson plans and teaching materials, create differentiated worksheets for students at different reading levels, produce quiz questions and assessment items aligned with specific learning objectives, translate content for multilingual classrooms within seconds, summarize long texts into student-appropriate versions, brainstorm project ideas, discussion questions, or creative activities, and draft communications to parents and colleagues.

This is genuinely new territory. Previous AI tools served narrow, specific purposes — a grammar checker could only check grammar. Large language models can assist with a remarkably wide range of tasks, which makes them both more useful and more important to understand well.

The quality of AI-generated content depends heavily on how you ask for it. A vague request like "make a worksheet" produces generic, often unusable output. A specific request like "create a 10-question vocabulary worksheet on photosynthesis for 7th-grade students, with definitions to match and two application questions" produces something much closer to what you actually need. This skill — communicating clearly with AI — is explored in depth in the Practical AI Skills lessons.

## Teacher Competencies That Transfer to AI

One of the most important messages of this course is that **effective teaching skills translate directly to effective AI use**. You are not starting from zero. The professional competencies you have built over your career are precisely the ones that matter most when working with AI.

| Teaching Skill | How It Applies to AI |
|---|---|
| Writing clear learning objectives | Writing clear, specific prompts that produce useful AI outputs |
| Differentiating instruction for diverse learners | Asking AI to adapt content for different levels, languages, or needs |
| Assessing and evaluating student work | Evaluating AI-generated content for accuracy, quality, and appropriateness |
| Giving specific, actionable feedback | Providing specific follow-up instructions to improve AI output |
| Scaffolding complex tasks into steps | Breaking complex AI requests into manageable, sequential steps |
| Reflecting on lesson effectiveness | Iterating on AI interactions to improve results over time |

This parallel is not a coincidence. Both teaching and working with AI require you to be clear about your goals, specific about your expectations, and critical about the results. The better you are at teaching, the better you will be at using AI — and vice versa.

## What Is Actually Changing?

It is worth being honest about what AI is and is not changing in education. Here is a balanced view:

**What is changing:** The speed at which teachers can create and adapt materials. The range of tasks AI can assist with. The expectations society has about AI in schools. The skills students need to navigate a world where AI is ubiquitous. The conversations teachers need to have about academic integrity, source evaluation, and the nature of knowledge.

**What is not changing:** The central importance of the teacher-student relationship. The need for professional judgment in pedagogical decisions. The value of human creativity, empathy, and moral reasoning. The fact that learning is fundamentally a social and emotional process, not just an information transfer.

> **Key Insight**: AI changes the tools available to teachers, not the purpose of teaching. The teachers who thrive in this transition will not be those who know the most about technology — they will be those who understand how to integrate new tools into sound pedagogical practice.

## Starting Points: Where to Begin

If you are just beginning to explore AI in your teaching practice, here are practical suggestions for getting started:

**Try a single task first.** Choose one routine task that takes you significant time — writing quiz questions, adapting a text for lower reading levels, or drafting a parent communication — and try using AI for it. Evaluate the result honestly: Was it useful? Where did it need improvement?

**Compare AI output to your own work.** Generate a lesson plan draft with AI and compare it to one you would write yourself. The differences will teach you both what AI does well and where your expertise adds irreplaceable value.

**Talk to colleagues.** Find out how other teachers in your school are using AI. Sharing experiences — both positive and negative — accelerates everyone's learning.

**Be patient with yourself.** Learning to work effectively with AI is a new professional skill. It takes practice, experimentation, and time. You do not need to master it overnight.

## Summary

- AI is already present in education through adaptive learning platforms, automated assessment tools, writing assistants, and recommendation algorithms — it is not entirely new
- The biggest recent change is the arrival of large language models that can assist with a wide range of content creation tasks through natural language conversation
- Automated assessment is useful for routine tasks but cannot replace the professional judgment of a teacher
- Teaching skills — clarity, specificity, evaluation, iteration — transfer directly to working with AI effectively
- AI changes the tools available to teachers, not the purpose of teaching
- The best starting point is choosing one routine task, trying AI for it, and honestly evaluating the results
