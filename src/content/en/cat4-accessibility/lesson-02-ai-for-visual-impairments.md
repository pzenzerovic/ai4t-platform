## When Seeing the Board Is Not Enough

In most classrooms, a significant portion of teaching happens visually. Teachers write on the board, project slides, distribute printed worksheets, and point to diagrams in textbooks. For students with full vision, this visual channel is so natural that it becomes invisible — they do not think about the fact that they are reading the board because reading the board is effortless. For students with visual impairments, every one of these moments represents a potential barrier.

The challenges vary widely. Some students have low vision and can see large text but not small print. Others have conditions that affect contrast sensitivity, making it difficult to read light text on a white board. Some students are legally blind and rely entirely on auditory and tactile information. In each case, the visual content that teachers present must be made available through alternative channels — and traditionally, this has required either an assistant who reads aloud, pre-prepared enlarged materials, or Braille transcriptions that take time and resources to produce.

AI tools — particularly those with image analysis and voice capabilities — offer a new approach. They do not replace the established support systems, but they provide an immediate, flexible alternative that students can use independently in many situations. This lesson explores two key AI capabilities for visually impaired students: image analysis in real time and voice-based interaction.

## Image Analysis in Real Time

One of the most concrete applications of AI in accessibility is *image analysis* — sometimes called *Vision mode* in tools like ChatGPT. This functionality allows an AI system to "see" and interpret images, including photographs taken by a student's smartphone.

The practical application is straightforward. A student with low vision points their phone camera at the board, a textbook page, or a worksheet. They take a photograph and upload it to an AI tool. The AI analyses the image and can perform several useful tasks: it can read all text visible in the image, describe visual elements such as diagrams or charts, explain the structure and layout of the content, and answer follow-up questions about what it sees.

This goes significantly beyond simple optical character recognition, which has existed for decades. Traditional OCR extracts raw text from an image but does nothing to organise, explain, or contextualise it. AI-powered image analysis, by contrast, can understand the structure of what it sees. If a student photographs a board that contains a diagram with labels and a set of equations, the AI can describe the diagram, read the equations, explain how they relate to each other, and structure the entire content into clear notes — all from a single photograph.

> **Key Insight**: AI image analysis is not just text recognition — it is content understanding. A student who photographs the board does not merely receive a transcript of what is written there. They receive an organised, structured interpretation that can be adapted to their specific needs.

### Practical Classroom Scenarios

Consider a typical lesson in which the teacher explains a new concept while writing key points and drawing a diagram on the board. A sighted student copies the notes and sketches the diagram. A student with low vision might miss half of what is written, struggle to distinguish the diagram's elements, and fall behind while trying to decipher what they can partially see.

With AI image analysis, the same student can photograph the board at the end of the explanation and prompt the AI with a request such as: "Describe in detail what you see on this image and structure the content into clear notes." The AI returns a complete, organised version of the board content — text, diagram description, and relationships between elements — that the student can read at their preferred text size on their device.

The student can then ask follow-up questions to deepen their understanding: "What does the diagram show?" or "Explain the relationship between the equation on the left and the one on the right." This creates an interactive learning experience that adapts to the student's needs in ways that a static enlargement cannot.

Another common scenario involves worksheets and printed materials. A student who receives a worksheet with small print can photograph it and ask the AI to read the content, explain the instructions, or even describe any images or diagrams on the sheet. This is particularly valuable for materials that were not prepared with accessibility in mind — which, in practice, describes the majority of everyday classroom handouts.

Laboratory settings offer yet another application. A student in a science class who cannot clearly see a demonstration or an experimental setup can photograph it and ask the AI to describe what is visible: "What equipment is on the table? How is it arranged? What does the display on the measuring device show?" This level of description provides access to visual information that would otherwise require a human assistant to narrate.

## Voice-Based Interaction

The second major AI capability for visually impaired students is *voice mode* — the ability to interact with AI through speech rather than text. In tools like ChatGPT, voice mode allows the student to speak into the device's microphone and receive a spoken response, creating a conversational interaction that requires no reading or typing.

For students with significant visual impairments, voice interaction removes the barrier of the text-based interface itself. Instead of typing a prompt and reading a response, the student simply speaks: "Explain photosynthesis to me in simple terms." The AI responds with a spoken explanation. The student can then ask follow-up questions: "Can you explain that more simply?" or "Give me an example from everyday life." The entire interaction happens through audio, making it fully accessible without any visual component.

This capability is particularly powerful when combined with image analysis. A student can photograph the board, then switch to voice mode and say: "Read me what is on the board and explain the main ideas." The AI processes the image and delivers its analysis as spoken text. The student receives the full content of the board without having seen it, and can ask clarifying questions in real time.

> **Tip**: Voice mode is especially useful during lessons when the student needs to keep pace with the class. Rather than stopping to type a prompt and read a response, the student can have a brief spoken exchange with the AI — similar to whispering a question to a classmate — and receive the information they need without disrupting their participation in the ongoing lesson.

### Beyond Reading Aloud

It is worth emphasising that AI voice interaction is fundamentally different from traditional text-to-speech tools. Text-to-speech converts written text into audio — a valuable function, but a limited one. AI voice interaction is a conversation. The student does not merely listen to content; they engage with it. They can ask the AI to explain a concept differently, to use simpler language, to provide examples, or to test their understanding by asking questions.

This conversational dimension transforms AI from a reading aid into a learning partner. A student using traditional text-to-speech hears a text read aloud but cannot ask "What does this mean?" A student using AI voice interaction can ask exactly that — and receive an explanation tailored to their level of understanding, followed by further questions if needed. The interaction mirrors the kind of one-on-one tutoring that is educationally ideal but practically impossible for teachers to provide to every student simultaneously.

## Limitations and Considerations

Despite these capabilities, AI image analysis and voice interaction have important limitations that teachers should understand.

Image quality matters significantly. A photograph taken from a distance, at an angle, or in poor lighting will produce less accurate results. Students need to learn practical skills such as taking clear, well-lit photographs and cropping images to focus on the relevant content. These are simple skills, but they require explicit instruction — particularly for students who may not be able to see clearly whether the photograph they have taken is adequate.

AI descriptions of visual content are not always accurate. Complex diagrams, handwritten text, and images with unusual layouts can be misinterpreted. Teachers should encourage students to verify AI descriptions against other sources when possible, and should be prepared to correct errors when they review the student's adapted materials.

Privacy and classroom norms must be considered. A student who photographs the board is also potentially photographing classmates, the teacher, or other content visible in the classroom. Schools should establish clear guidelines about when and how photography for AI-assisted learning is permitted, and students should understand these boundaries.

Finally, voice interaction requires a quiet environment or headphones. In a busy classroom, spoken AI responses can be distracting to other students. Headphones solve this problem but add another device to manage. Teachers should plan for these practical considerations when integrating voice-based AI tools into their classroom routines.

> **Warning**: AI image analysis can misread handwritten text, misinterpret diagrams, or miss elements that are partially obscured. Every AI-generated description of visual content should be treated as an approximation that may need correction — not as a guaranteed accurate transcript. The teacher's role in verifying and supplementing AI output remains essential.

## What This Means for Your Classroom

You do not need specialised assistive technology to begin using AI for visually impaired students. A smartphone with access to ChatGPT or a similar AI tool provides both image analysis and voice interaction capabilities. The student photographs content, uploads it, and receives a description — or speaks a question and hears an answer. The technology is already in most students' pockets.

The practical steps are modest. Allow students who need visual assistance to use their phones during lessons for AI-supported learning. Teach them effective prompts for image analysis: "Describe everything you see in this image," "Read the text and organise it into notes," "Explain the diagram step by step." Establish classroom norms about photography and voice interaction. And review the AI's output periodically to ensure accuracy and pedagogical appropriateness.

These adjustments do not require restructuring your teaching. They require awareness that the tools exist, willingness to allow their use, and occasional oversight to ensure quality. For many students with visual impairments, this modest investment of attention can significantly expand their access to everyday classroom content.

## Summary

- AI image analysis allows students with visual impairments to photograph boards, textbooks, worksheets, and other visual content and receive structured, organised descriptions that go beyond simple text recognition.
- Voice-based AI interaction removes the barrier of text-based interfaces entirely, allowing students to speak questions and receive spoken answers in a conversational format that supports follow-up and clarification.
- Combining image analysis with voice interaction creates a powerful accessibility workflow: the student photographs content and then engages with it through spoken dialogue, accessing visual information without needing to see it.
- AI voice interaction differs fundamentally from traditional text-to-speech because it is conversational — students can ask for explanations, simpler language, examples, or comprehension checks, transforming the AI from a reading aid into a learning partner.
- Practical limitations include image quality dependence, potential inaccuracies in describing complex diagrams or handwriting, privacy considerations around classroom photography, and the need for headphones in shared spaces.
- Teachers do not need specialised equipment to implement these tools — a smartphone with access to a modern AI platform provides both image analysis and voice capabilities that can be used immediately.
- The teacher's role remains essential in verifying AI-generated descriptions, establishing classroom norms for AI-assisted accessibility, and ensuring that the adapted content serves the learning objectives accurately.
