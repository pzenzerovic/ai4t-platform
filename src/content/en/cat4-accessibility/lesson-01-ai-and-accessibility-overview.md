## Why Accessibility Is Every Teacher's Concern

When teachers hear the word "accessibility," they often think of specialised equipment, dedicated support staff, or bureaucratic procedures that take weeks to arrange. In many schools, that is still the reality: a student who needs adapted materials must wait for an assistant, a printed enlargement, or a translation that may arrive days after the lesson has passed. The gap between the moment a barrier appears and the moment help arrives is where learning is lost.

Artificial intelligence does not replace the systems of support that schools have built over decades. It does not perform pedagogical diagnosis, design individualised curricula, or provide the emotional and social support that every student needs. But it can do something that no previous technology could do at this scale: it can reduce specific, concrete barriers in the moment they arise, without requiring additional equipment, specialised training, or advance planning.

This lesson introduces the four core areas of accessibility where AI can make an immediate difference, explains the underlying principle that connects them, and sets the stage for the detailed lessons that follow. By the end of this category, you will have a practical understanding of how AI tools can support students with visual impairments, hearing impairments, cognitive processing difficulties, language barriers, and intellectual disabilities — and you will know the boundaries of what these tools can and cannot do.

## What Accessibility Means in Practice

Accessibility in education means enabling every student to participate in the learning process regardless of their functional differences. This includes students with impaired vision or hearing, students with learning difficulties such as dyslexia or attention deficit disorders, students with intellectual disabilities, and students who do not speak the language of instruction. Each of these groups encounters different barriers, but the barriers share a common characteristic: they prevent the student from accessing the same content that their peers can access freely.

Traditionally, addressing these barriers has required significant resources. Schools need teaching assistants, specialised aids, adapted materials, additional preparation time, and the involvement of professional support staff. These needs have not disappeared, and AI does not eliminate them. What AI does is provide faster, simpler, and more readily available ways to adapt content at the moment it is needed.

For a student who cannot see well, this might mean instantly "reading" text from the board without waiting for an assistant. For a student who cannot hear well, it might be an automatic transcription of the teacher's explanation. For a student who struggles with complex text, it might be a simplified version of the same content produced in seconds. For a student who does not speak the language of instruction, it might be a translation of instructions or an explanation in their mother tongue. In each case, the AI does not solve the underlying condition — it bridges the gap between the barrier and the support.

> **Key Insight**: AI does not make education automatically inclusive. What it does is act as a bridge between the moment a barrier appears and the moment professional help arrives. That bridge, however small, can mean the difference between a student participating in a lesson and a student being left behind.

## The Four Core Areas

The lessons in this category are organised around four fundamental areas of accessibility, each of which represents a distinct type of barrier that students encounter in everyday classroom settings.

The first area is vision. Students with visual impairments face difficulty reading text on the board, in textbooks, or on worksheets. AI tools with image analysis capabilities can photograph and interpret visual content, describe what they see, read text aloud, and restructure information into accessible formats. The next lesson in this category explores this area in detail, including practical scenarios that you can implement with tools already available on most smartphones.

The second area is hearing. Students with hearing impairments miss spoken content — teacher explanations, class discussions, audio materials, and verbal instructions. AI-powered transcription tools can convert speech to text in real time, and large language models can then structure, summarise, and simplify those transcripts into effective study materials. This transforms raw audio into organised, reviewable text that the student can study at their own pace.

The third area is cognitive processing. This is perhaps the broadest area of application. Students with dyslexia, ADHD, autism spectrum conditions, or other cognitive processing differences may struggle with texts that are too complex, too long, or too densely structured. AI can simplify language, break content into smaller chunks, create structured outlines, and present the same information in multiple formats — all without changing the learning objectives themselves.

The fourth area is language. In increasingly diverse classrooms across Europe, many students do not speak the language of instruction fluently. AI translation and language adaptation tools can provide near-instant translations, adjust the complexity of language to match the student's proficiency level, and allow students to interact with content in their mother tongue while they continue learning the language of instruction.

A fifth area, covered in its own dedicated lesson, addresses students with intellectual disabilities. This requires a different approach because AI tools in this context are used exclusively under mentor guidance, with specific rules about autonomy, complexity, and the role of technology in the learning process.

## The Underlying Principle: AI as a Real-Time Adaptation Tool

Across all four areas, the same underlying principle applies. AI is most valuable in accessibility not as a replacement for professional support, but as a tool for real-time adaptation. The key word is "real-time." Traditional adaptations — enlarged texts, pre-prepared translations, simplified worksheets — require planning in advance. A teacher must know what adaptations are needed, prepare them before the lesson, and have them ready when the student arrives. This works for planned content, but it does not work for spontaneous explanations, class discussions, new examples given on the board, or any content that emerges during the lesson itself.

AI changes this dynamic because it can adapt content in the moment. A student can photograph a board and receive a structured summary. A student can record an explanation and receive a transcript. A student can paste a complex text and receive a simplified version. None of these require advance preparation by the teacher. They happen at the point of need, which is precisely when they are most valuable.

This does not mean that advance planning becomes unnecessary. A teacher who prepares adapted materials in advance will always provide a better learning experience than one who relies entirely on real-time AI adaptation. But for the many moments in a school day when prepared adaptations are not available — when a new topic is introduced, when a student asks a question that leads the lesson in an unexpected direction, when a worksheet from another class is used without adaptation — AI provides a safety net that did not exist before.

> **Tip**: You do not need to become an expert in assistive technology to use AI for accessibility. Most of the tools described in this category work through the same AI platforms you may already use for other purposes — ChatGPT, Claude, or similar tools. The difference is in how you prompt them and what you ask them to do.

## What AI Cannot Do

It is equally important to understand the boundaries of AI in accessibility. AI cannot assess a student's individual needs. It cannot determine whether a simplification is appropriate or excessive. It cannot provide the emotional support, patience, and human connection that students with disabilities need. It cannot replace a trained special education professional, a speech therapist, or a school psychologist.

AI also does not know the optimal level of adaptation. When you ask it to simplify a text, it simplifies according to the instruction — but it cannot judge whether the simplification is pedagogically appropriate for a specific student. That judgement belongs to the teacher. The teacher decides when AI is suitable, how it is used, what level of adaptation is appropriate, and when the technology should be set aside entirely.

Furthermore, AI tools are not infallible. Transcriptions contain errors. Translations miss nuance. Simplifications can lose important meaning. Image descriptions can be incomplete or inaccurate. Every AI output in an accessibility context should be reviewed — either by the teacher or, where appropriate, by the student themselves as part of the learning process.

> **Warning**: AI is a powerful tool for accessibility, but its value depends entirely on how it is used and with what purpose. Without pedagogical intention, without understanding of the student's needs, and without human oversight, AI can become a source of confusion, overload, or even additional barriers. The teacher remains the essential decision-maker in every accessibility scenario.

## The Teacher's Role in Accessible AI Use

The teacher's role in AI-supported accessibility can be understood through three functions. First, the teacher is a gatekeeper who determines when AI assistance is appropriate and when it is not. Not every student needs AI adaptation, and not every moment calls for technological intervention. The teacher makes this judgement based on their knowledge of the student, the content, and the learning objectives.

Second, the teacher is a quality controller who reviews AI-generated adaptations before they reach the student — or who teaches the student to review them critically. A simplified text that loses essential meaning is not an adaptation; it is a distortion. A translation that introduces errors is not helpful; it is misleading. The teacher ensures that the adapted content still serves the learning objective.

Third, the teacher is a learning designer who integrates AI accessibility tools into the broader pedagogical framework. This means deciding at which points in the lesson AI assistance is available, what the student is expected to do independently, and how the adapted content connects to the standard curriculum. The goal is not to create a separate learning path for students with accessibility needs, but to use AI to reduce barriers so that all students can engage with the same learning objectives.

## A Starting Point, Not a Complete Solution

This category does not claim that AI solves the problem of educational accessibility. The challenges of inclusive education are systemic, institutional, and deeply human. They require policy, funding, training, and cultural change that no technology can provide on its own.

What this category does offer is a practical starting point. It shows you what is possible today, with tools that already exist, that you can use without additional equipment or technical expertise. It demonstrates specific scenarios — a student photographing a board, a student receiving a live transcript, a student getting a simplified version of a complex text — that you can try in your own classroom this week.

The tools are not perfect. They will not work in every situation. But they represent a meaningful step forward in making everyday classroom moments more accessible to students who have, until now, been waiting for help that often arrives too late.

## Summary

- Accessibility in education means enabling every student to participate in learning regardless of functional differences — and AI can reduce specific barriers in real time without requiring specialised equipment or advance planning.
- AI supports accessibility in four core areas: vision (reading and interpreting visual content), hearing (transcribing spoken content), cognitive processing (simplifying and restructuring text), and language (translating and adapting language level).
- The key advantage of AI in accessibility is real-time adaptation — the ability to modify content at the moment of need, which complements but does not replace advance preparation by teachers.
- AI cannot assess individual student needs, determine whether an adaptation is pedagogically appropriate, or provide emotional and social support — these responsibilities belong to the teacher and professional support staff.
- The teacher serves three essential functions in AI-supported accessibility: gatekeeper (deciding when AI is appropriate), quality controller (reviewing adapted content), and learning designer (integrating AI tools into the pedagogical framework).
- AI outputs in accessibility contexts are not infallible — transcriptions, translations, and simplifications can contain errors and must always be reviewed by the teacher or, where appropriate, by the student.
- This category provides a practical starting point for using existing AI tools to make everyday classroom moments more accessible, while acknowledging that true inclusion requires systemic change that technology alone cannot deliver.
