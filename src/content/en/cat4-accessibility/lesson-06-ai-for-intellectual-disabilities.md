## A Different Approach to AI in Education

The previous lessons in this category addressed accessibility needs where students can use AI tools with varying degrees of independence. A student with low vision can photograph the board and interact with the AI on their own. A student learning a new language can translate a worksheet without assistance. But when working with students with intellectual disabilities, the dynamic changes fundamentally. AI tools in this context are not used by the student alone — they are used by the mentor or teacher as a support tool, with the student always present and guided.

This distinction is not a minor procedural detail. It reflects a core principle of working with students with intellectual disabilities: technology serves the relationship between the mentor and the student, not as a replacement for it. The human connection — the patience, the encouragement, the ability to read frustration or confusion in a student's face — is irreplaceable. AI cannot provide emotional support, build trust, or adapt its approach based on the subtle non-verbal cues that an experienced mentor recognises intuitively.

What AI can do is help the mentor prepare, adapt, and deliver content more efficiently and consistently. It can simplify language faster than a mentor can rephrase it in the moment. It can break a complex task into steps more systematically. It can generate clear, sequential instructions that the mentor can use as a visual checklist. In each case, the AI is a tool in the mentor's hands — never a tool that operates independently with the student.

> **Key Insight**: When working with students with intellectual disabilities, AI serves the mentor, not the student directly. The student works with the mentor; the mentor works with AI. This layered approach preserves the human relationship that is essential for learning while leveraging AI's ability to adapt content quickly and consistently.

## The Principle: Adapt the Method, Not the Goal

The foundational principle of using AI for students with intellectual disabilities is that AI may adapt the method of learning but never the goal. The learning objective remains the same — only the pathway to achieving it changes.

This means AI tools may simplify the language of an instruction, break a task into smaller steps, provide additional explanations, or create visual supports. But they may not change what the student is expected to learn or do. They may not complete tasks on the student's behalf. And they may not provide ready-made answers that bypass the student's own effort and engagement.

This principle directly mirrors the central message of Category 3 in this course: AI should support the learning process, not replace it. For students with intellectual disabilities, this principle is even more critical because the temptation to use AI as a shortcut is compounded by the genuine difficulty of the tasks these students face. When a student struggles with a task, the most helpful-seeming response — letting AI do the work — is actually the most harmful, because it removes the opportunity for the student to develop their own capabilities, however gradually.

The mentor's role is to use AI to make the task accessible without making it unnecessary. A student who is learning to write their name does not need AI to write it for them. They may need AI to help the mentor create a clear, large-print tracing template, or to break the task into steps ("First write the letter P, then write the letter A"), or to generate encouraging prompts that the mentor can use during practice. The product — the name written by the student — must be the student's own work. The AI supports the scaffolding around that work.

## How AI Helps in Practice

The practical applications of AI for students with intellectual disabilities centre on three core functions: simplifying instructions, breaking tasks into steps, and creating visual and structural supports.

### Simplifying Instructions

Many educational activities involve instructions that contain multiple steps, abstract language, or implicit assumptions about what the student already knows. For students with intellectual disabilities, these instructions can be overwhelming. A workshop instruction like "Select appropriate materials and create a collage representing the four seasons" contains multiple embedded tasks, abstract concepts, and unstated assumptions.

A mentor can use AI to reformulate such instructions into clear, concrete language: "Take the green paper. Cut out a tree shape. Glue it on the big paper." Each instruction contains one action, uses concrete nouns, and employs action verbs that leave no ambiguity. The AI produces these reformulations in seconds, allowing the mentor to focus on guiding the student through the task rather than spending time translating complex instructions into accessible language.

### Breaking Tasks into Steps

For students with intellectual disabilities, a task that appears large or complex often triggers avoidance — the student feels that the task is impossible and disengages before starting. AI can help the mentor decompose any task into small, achievable steps that the student can complete one at a time.

Consider the task of making a poster. For a typical student, this might be a single assignment. For a student with intellectual disabilities, the mentor might break it into five steps: choose a picture, write a title (one word), add one sentence, colour the border, and save the work. The mentor can present these steps as a visual checklist, and the student checks off each step as it is completed. The task that felt impossible becomes a series of small victories, each building the student's confidence and motivation.

AI is particularly useful here because it can generate these step-by-step breakdowns for any task, in any subject, adapted to the specific level of the student. The mentor describes the task and the student's level, and the AI produces a structured sequence that the mentor can then use as a guide. Tools like Goblin Tools are specifically designed for this kind of task decomposition, but general AI tools like ChatGPT or Claude can perform the same function when prompted appropriately.

> **Tip**: When using AI to break tasks into steps for students with intellectual disabilities, follow the rule of one: one action per step, one piece of information per instruction, one tool at a time. Multiple elements in a single step create decision points that can overwhelm the student and lead to disengagement.

### Creating Visual and Structural Supports

Students with intellectual disabilities often respond more effectively to visual supports than to text-based instructions. AI can help mentors create simple visual sequences, icon-based instructions, and structured daily routines that provide the predictability and clarity these students need.

While AI cannot generate pictograms directly (specialised tools like ARASAAC are designed for that purpose), it can create text-based visual structures: numbered step lists, structured daily schedules, and clear before-and-after sequences that the mentor can pair with appropriate images or symbols. The combination of AI-generated structure with visual elements from pictogram libraries creates powerful instructional materials that are both accessible and consistent.

Consistency is particularly important for students with intellectual disabilities. When the same type of task always follows the same structure, the student develops routines that reduce anxiety and increase independence. AI helps the mentor maintain this consistency by generating materials in the same format every time, even when the specific content changes.

## Rules for AI Use in Mentor-Guided Work

Working with students with intellectual disabilities requires strict guidelines for how AI is used. These guidelines protect the student and ensure that technology serves its intended purpose of supporting — not replacing — the mentor-student relationship.

The first and most fundamental rule is that the student does not use AI independently. All AI interactions are conducted by the mentor, and the outputs are reviewed and adapted by the mentor before being shared with the student. The student works with the mentor, who may use AI in the background to prepare or adapt materials. This is not about restricting the student's autonomy unnecessarily — it is about recognising that unsupervised interaction with an AI tool that generates large amounts of text, makes suggestions, and asks questions can be confusing, distracting, or even distressing for students who are not equipped to evaluate or manage that interaction.

The second rule is that less is more. In this context, the principle of minimal effective intervention applies with particular force. One tool per activity. One purpose per interaction. Short, focused use rather than continuous engagement. The goal is to use AI to prepare the right material and then step away from the technology, conducting the actual learning interaction in the human space between the mentor and the student.

The third rule is that the mentor filters all AI output. Everything that AI generates is reviewed by the mentor before it reaches the student. The mentor shortens, simplifies, and connects the AI output to the specific activity. If the output contains abstract language, too much information, or anything that might confuse the student, the mentor edits it or discards it entirely. The student sees only the final, mentor-curated version.

The fourth rule is that AI is never used for assessment. AI tools do not evaluate the student's progress, judge their abilities, or produce reports about their performance. Assessment of students with intellectual disabilities requires the nuanced observation of a human professional who understands the student's individual context, history, and developmental trajectory. AI has no capacity for this kind of assessment and should never be used in this role.

> **Warning**: If the mentor observes that the student becomes anxious, loses focus, or begins paying more attention to the device than to the activity, the AI tool should be turned off immediately. The learning continues through traditional mentor-student interaction. Technology that disrupts the learning relationship is not serving its purpose, regardless of how useful it might be in theory.

## The Mentor's Role

The mentor working with students with intellectual disabilities occupies a role that is fundamentally different from the teacher's role in other accessibility contexts. In other lessons in this category, the teacher designs the conditions for AI use and the student operates within those conditions with some degree of independence. Here, the mentor is present at every stage, actively mediating between the AI tool and the student.

This mediation involves several specific functions. The mentor selects which AI tool to use and for what purpose. The mentor formulates the prompts — the student does not interact with the AI directly. The mentor reviews the output and decides what to share with the student and how to present it. The mentor observes the student's response and adjusts in real time, using the AI-generated material as one resource among many in their instructional toolkit.

The mentor also decides when not to use AI. Not every learning moment benefits from technological support. Sometimes the most effective intervention is a quiet conversation, a physical demonstration, a shared activity, or simply patience while the student works through a challenge at their own pace. The mentor's professional judgement about when technology helps and when it hinders is the most important factor in the successful use of AI for students with intellectual disabilities.

## Safety and Privacy

Working with students with intellectual disabilities involves heightened privacy and safety obligations. Mentors must be particularly careful about the information they share with AI tools. Personal data about the student — their name, diagnosis, specific difficulties, or any identifying information — should never be entered into an AI platform. Prompts should describe the need in general terms: "Simplify this text for a student with limited reading ability" rather than "Simplify this text for [student name] who has [specific diagnosis]."

Additionally, mentors should not use AI tools to record, store, or share information about the student's behaviour, progress, or challenges without explicit permission from the appropriate guardians and in compliance with school data protection policies. AI tools are content adaptation tools in this context — nothing more.

## Summary

- AI tools for students with intellectual disabilities are used exclusively by the mentor, not independently by the student — the mentor mediates all interactions between the technology and the student, preserving the human relationship that is essential for learning.
- The foundational principle is that AI adapts the method of learning, never the goal — simplifying instructions and breaking tasks into steps is appropriate, but providing ready-made answers or completing tasks on the student's behalf is not.
- The three core functions of AI in this context are simplifying instructions into clear, concrete language, decomposing tasks into small achievable steps, and creating structured visual supports that provide predictability and reduce anxiety.
- Strict rules govern AI use: the student does not use AI independently, less is more (one tool, one purpose, short duration), the mentor filters all AI output before it reaches the student, and AI is never used for assessment.
- If the student becomes anxious, disengaged, or distracted by the technology, the AI tool should be turned off immediately and learning should continue through direct mentor-student interaction.
- Privacy obligations are heightened — mentors must never enter personal student data into AI tools and should describe needs in general terms rather than sharing identifying information.
- The mentor's professional judgement about when to use AI, how to adapt its output, and when to set it aside entirely is the most critical factor in successful implementation.
