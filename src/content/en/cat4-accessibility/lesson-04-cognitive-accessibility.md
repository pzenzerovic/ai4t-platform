## The Widest Area of Need

Of all the accessibility challenges that teachers face, cognitive accessibility may be the most common and the most difficult to address systematically. Visual and hearing impairments are relatively easy to identify — a student either can or cannot see the board, can or cannot hear the teacher. Cognitive processing difficulties, by contrast, exist on a broad and often invisible spectrum. A student may be able to read every word on a page and still not understand the paragraph. The text is accessible in a physical sense but inaccessible in a cognitive one.

This category includes students with a wide range of conditions: dyslexia, which affects the processing of written language; dyscalculia, which affects numerical processing; attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, which affects sustained focus and working memory; autism spectrum conditions, which can affect the processing of complex or ambiguous information; and a range of other learning difficulties that make standard educational materials harder to process than they need to be. It also includes students without a diagnosed condition who simply find certain texts too dense, too abstract, or too poorly structured to understand on first reading.

The traditional response to cognitive accessibility has been differentiation — preparing multiple versions of the same material at different complexity levels. This is pedagogically sound but practically exhausting. A teacher who creates three versions of every worksheet, every reading passage, and every set of instructions is doing three times the preparation work. In practice, most teachers differentiate some of the time, for some students, in some subjects. Comprehensive, real-time differentiation has been beyond the practical capacity of individual teachers.

AI changes this equation fundamentally. It can adapt the complexity of any text, in any subject, for any level, in seconds. This does not eliminate the need for thoughtful pedagogical design, but it removes the bottleneck that has made comprehensive differentiation impractical for most teachers.

> **Key Insight**: Cognitive accessibility is not about lowering standards or reducing expectations. It is about presenting the same content in a way that the student's cognitive processing can handle. The learning objective remains the same — only the path to understanding changes.

## What Cognitive Adaptation Looks Like

AI tools for cognitive adaptation work through text transformation. The teacher or student provides a text — a paragraph from a textbook, a set of instructions, a problem statement — and the AI modifies it according to specific instructions. The range of possible transformations is broad, and each serves a different accessibility purpose.

The most straightforward transformation is simplification. A complex sentence with multiple clauses, technical vocabulary, and abstract concepts can be rephrased into shorter sentences with everyday language and concrete examples. This is not the same as "dumbing down" the content — it is restructuring it so that the essential meaning is preserved while the cognitive demands of processing the language are reduced. A student with dyslexia who struggles to parse a fifty-word sentence may understand the same idea perfectly when it is expressed in three fifteen-word sentences.

Structural transformation is equally powerful. A dense paragraph of continuous text can be converted into a numbered list of steps, a table with clear columns, or a set of short sections with descriptive headings. For students with attention difficulties, the visual structure of the text matters as much as the words themselves. A block of text without structure provides no cognitive anchors — nowhere for the eye to rest, no way to identify what is most important. A structured version of the same content provides a clear path through the information.

Concrete examples and analogies represent another form of cognitive adaptation. Abstract concepts are inherently harder to process than concrete ones. When a text explains a principle in abstract terms, an AI tool can add concrete examples, everyday analogies, or visual descriptions that make the abstraction more accessible. This is not a simplification — it is an enrichment that reduces the cognitive distance between the student and the concept.

## Practical Prompts for Teachers

The effectiveness of AI cognitive adaptation depends heavily on the specificity of the prompt. A vague request like "Make this simpler" produces unpredictable results. Specific, targeted prompts produce consistent, useful output. Here are several approaches that teachers and students can use, each suited to different needs and situations.

For general simplification, a teacher might prompt: "Rewrite this text so that it is understandable for a student with reading difficulties. Use short sentences, everyday vocabulary, and concrete examples." This produces a version that maintains the core content while reducing linguistic complexity.

For structural reorganisation, the prompt might be: "Convert this text into a table with three columns: concept, explanation, and example." Tables impose a visual structure that helps students with attention difficulties focus on one piece of information at a time, and the parallel structure makes comparison and review easier.

For step-by-step conversion, particularly useful for instructions or procedures, the prompt might be: "Break this text into numbered steps, with each step containing only one action." Many students with cognitive processing difficulties become overwhelmed when instructions contain multiple actions in a single sentence. Separating each action into its own numbered step makes the task feel manageable rather than overwhelming.

For concept mapping, the prompt might be: "Create a text-based concept map showing how the ideas in this text relate to each other." This helps students who struggle with linear text but process relational information more effectively when they can see connections between ideas.

> **Tip**: Create a short reference sheet of effective prompts for cognitive adaptation and share it with students who need it. Having pre-written prompts removes the additional cognitive burden of figuring out how to ask for help, which can itself be a barrier for students with processing difficulties.

## The Teacher's Judgement Remains Essential

AI adapts content according to instructions, but it cannot judge whether the adaptation is appropriate. This is a critical distinction. An AI tool will simplify a text as much as you ask it to — but it has no way of knowing whether the result is still challenging enough to promote learning, or whether it has been simplified to the point where the educational value is lost.

Consider a biology text about cell division. An appropriate simplification for a student with dyslexia might involve shorter sentences and clearer vocabulary while preserving all the scientific concepts. An inappropriate simplification might strip out essential terminology and reduce the content to a level below the curriculum standard. The AI cannot tell the difference — it simply follows the instruction. The teacher must review the output and decide whether the adaptation serves the learning objective or undermines it.

This is why cognitive adaptation works best as a collaborative process between the teacher and the AI tool, rather than a process the student manages entirely alone. The teacher defines the boundaries: how much simplification is appropriate, which technical terms must be preserved, what level of complexity is the target. Within those boundaries, the AI does the work of transformation quickly and consistently.

For students who are more independent, teachers can provide guidance in advance: "When you use AI to simplify texts, always keep the scientific terms — just ask for simpler explanations of what they mean." This kind of meta-instruction helps students use AI effectively while maintaining the pedagogical standards that the teacher has set.

> **Warning**: AI does not know which level of simplification is optimal for a specific student. It adapts content according to the instruction it receives, regardless of whether the result is pedagogically appropriate. The teacher must review AI-adapted materials and ensure they still meet the curriculum requirements and the student's actual learning needs.

## Specific Learning Difficulties

Different cognitive processing challenges benefit from different types of AI adaptation, and understanding these distinctions helps teachers provide more targeted support.

For students with dyslexia, the primary barriers are the visual processing of text and the decoding of written language. AI can help by reformatting text with shorter sentences, clearer paragraph breaks, and simpler vocabulary. Some students with dyslexia also benefit from having text read aloud — a function that AI voice capabilities can provide alongside the simplified text, creating a dual-channel (visual plus auditory) presentation that supports comprehension.

For students with ADHD, the primary barriers are sustained attention and working memory capacity. Long, unstructured texts are particularly challenging because they require the reader to hold information in working memory while processing new information. AI can help by breaking content into small, labelled sections, creating bullet-point summaries of key information, and structuring tasks into clear, sequential steps with checkpoints. The visual structure serves as an external support for the internal attention processes that the student finds difficult.

For students on the autism spectrum, the barriers may include difficulty processing ambiguous language, implicit social meaning, or text that assumes shared background knowledge. AI can help by making implicit information explicit, defining terms and expressions that might be unclear, and restructuring content to follow a predictable, logical pattern. Students with autism often excel when information is presented in structured, systematic formats — and AI is particularly effective at creating those formats.

For students with general learning difficulties who process information more slowly than their peers, AI can reduce the volume of text while preserving the essential content. A ten-page chapter can be condensed into a two-page summary that covers the key concepts. A complex problem statement can be rephrased in simpler language with an example. The goal is not to reduce the learning — it is to make the learning accessible within the student's processing capacity.

## Cognitive Adaptation Is Not Just for Students with Diagnoses

One of the most important aspects of cognitive adaptation through AI is that it benefits a much wider range of students than those with formal diagnoses. In any classroom, there are students who find a particular text confusing, a particular set of instructions unclear, or a particular concept too abstract. These students do not have diagnosed learning difficulties — they simply need the content presented differently in that moment.

AI makes it practical to provide this kind of in-the-moment adaptation for any student who needs it. A student who is struggling with a physics text can paste it into an AI tool and ask for a simpler explanation without needing a formal accommodation plan, a specialist referral, or additional teacher preparation time. This normalises the use of accessibility tools, reduces the stigma associated with needing "special" materials, and creates a classroom culture where adapting content to your needs is a skill rather than a sign of weakness.

This perspective is directly connected to the principles explored in Category 3 of this course. Using AI to make content more accessible is not the same as using AI to bypass the learning process. A student who asks AI to simplify a text and then engages with the simplified version is still doing the cognitive work of understanding — they are simply doing it through a channel that their brain can process more effectively. The learning objective is preserved; only the delivery is adapted.

## Summary

- Cognitive accessibility addresses the broadest range of learning needs — including dyslexia, ADHD, autism spectrum conditions, and general processing difficulties — where standard educational materials may be physically accessible but cognitively inaccessible.
- AI enables real-time text transformation: simplifying language, restructuring information into tables or numbered steps, adding concrete examples, and reducing the volume of text — all without changing the underlying learning objectives.
- Effective cognitive adaptation requires specific, targeted prompts rather than vague requests — prompts that specify the audience, the desired format, and which elements (such as technical vocabulary) must be preserved.
- The teacher's judgement is essential because AI cannot determine whether an adaptation is pedagogically appropriate — it simplifies as much as instructed, regardless of whether the result still meets curriculum standards.
- Different cognitive processing challenges benefit from different types of adaptation: reformatted text for dyslexia, structured sections for ADHD, explicit and systematic formats for autism, and condensed content for general processing difficulties.
- Cognitive adaptation through AI benefits all students, not just those with formal diagnoses — normalising the use of text adaptation tools reduces stigma and creates a classroom culture where adapting content to individual needs is a valued skill.
- Using AI to make content more cognitively accessible is fundamentally different from using AI to bypass learning — the student still engages with the content, but through a channel that their brain can process more effectively.
