## The Invisible Barrier of Spoken Content

Teaching is, in large part, a spoken activity. Teachers explain concepts, give instructions, ask questions, lead discussions, and provide feedback — all through speech. For most students, this auditory channel works seamlessly. They hear, they process, they respond. For students with hearing impairments, every spoken word represents a potential gap in their access to the lesson.

The spectrum of hearing impairment is wide. Some students have mild hearing loss and can follow most speech if the environment is quiet and the speaker faces them directly. Others have moderate loss and rely on hearing aids that amplify sound but do not always clarify it. Some students are profoundly deaf and depend entirely on sign language interpretation, lip reading, or written communication. In each case, the student's access to the teacher's spoken content is incomplete, delayed, or filtered through an intermediary.

Traditional accommodations for hearing-impaired students include sign language interpreters, FM systems that transmit the teacher's voice directly to the student's hearing aid, note-taking services provided by a classmate or assistant, and preferential seating near the teacher. These supports are valuable and should continue. But they share a common limitation: they are either expensive, require advance arrangement, or depend on the availability of another person. When the interpreter is absent, when the note-taker is not available, or when the student is in a class without arranged support, the barrier returns in full.

AI offers a complementary approach that addresses this gap. Through automatic speech-to-text transcription and subsequent text processing, AI can convert spoken content into structured, reviewable written material — in real time or shortly after the lesson. This does not replace human interpreters or professional support, but it provides a reliable backup and, in many cases, a primary tool that students can use independently.

## Real-Time Transcription

The foundation of AI-assisted hearing accessibility is *automatic speech recognition* — the ability to convert spoken language into written text as it is being spoken. Several tools now offer this capability with remarkable accuracy, and new services continue to emerge.

Dedicated transcription tools such as Otter.ai are designed specifically for this purpose. They record audio through a device's microphone and produce a running transcript in real time. The student can watch the text appear on their screen as the teacher speaks, following the lesson in written form rather than — or in addition to — auditory form. The transcript is saved automatically and can be reviewed after the lesson.

The practical setup is straightforward. The student places a phone or tablet on their desk with the transcription application running. As the teacher speaks, the words appear on screen with minimal delay. The student reads along, and after the lesson, they have a complete written record of everything that was said. For many students with hearing impairments, this is transformative — it provides access to the full content of the lesson, not just the portions they managed to hear or lip-read.

> **Key Insight**: Real-time transcription does not merely reproduce what the teacher says — it converts an ephemeral, time-bound medium (speech) into a permanent, reviewable medium (text). For a hearing-impaired student, the ability to go back and re-read something the teacher said five minutes ago is a fundamental change in how they access the lesson.

### Accuracy and Limitations

Transcription accuracy depends on several factors. Clear speech, moderate pace, and a reasonably quiet environment produce the best results. Background noise, multiple speakers talking simultaneously, heavy accents, and technical vocabulary can all reduce accuracy. In a typical classroom setting, modern transcription tools achieve accuracy rates that are useful but not perfect — the student will get the substance of what is being said, but individual words may be incorrect, and fast-paced exchanges during class discussions may be partially lost.

Teachers can significantly improve transcription accuracy through small adjustments to their practice. Speaking clearly and at a moderate pace, facing the class rather than the board while speaking, repeating student questions before answering them (so the question is captured in the transcript), and minimising background noise during important explanations — all of these make a measurable difference. These adjustments benefit not only the transcription tool but also any students who rely on hearing aids or lip reading.

## From Raw Transcript to Learning Material

A raw transcript is valuable, but it is often long, unstructured, and difficult to study from. Spoken language includes false starts, repetitions, digressions, and informal phrasing that work perfectly in conversation but create a messy written document. This is where large language models — ChatGPT, Claude, and similar tools — add their greatest value for hearing-impaired students.

After the lesson, the student can copy the transcript into an AI tool and transform it into effective study material through targeted prompts. The possibilities are extensive and each serves a different learning purpose.

For summarisation, the student might prompt: "Summarise this transcript into ten key points, organised by topic." The AI distills a forty-minute lecture into a concise, structured overview that captures the essential content without the digressions and repetitions of live speech.

For structured notes, the student might prompt: "Convert this transcript into organised notes with headings, subheadings, and key terms highlighted." The AI transforms the linear flow of speech into a hierarchical structure that is far easier to review and memorise.

For vocabulary extraction, particularly useful in technical subjects, the student might prompt: "Identify all specialised terms used in this transcript and provide a brief definition for each." The AI produces a glossary that the student can use for revision, ensuring that technical language — which is often the hardest to catch through impaired hearing — is clearly defined.

For simplification, particularly useful when the teacher's explanation was complex or rapid, the student might prompt: "Explain the main ideas from this transcript in simpler language, suitable for a student who missed parts of the explanation." The AI rephrases the content at an accessible level, filling in the gaps that the student's hearing loss may have created.

> **Tip**: Teach hearing-impaired students a standard post-lesson workflow: record the lesson using a transcription tool, then process the transcript with an AI tool using two or three standard prompts they have practised. Over time, this becomes a routine that takes only a few minutes but produces study materials of significantly higher quality than raw transcripts alone.

## Combining Transcription with AI Processing

The most effective workflow for hearing-impaired students combines real-time transcription with post-lesson AI processing. During the lesson, the transcription tool captures the spoken content. After the lesson, the AI tool transforms that content into structured, accessible learning material. This two-stage approach plays to the strengths of each technology: the transcription tool is fast and captures everything in real time; the AI tool is intelligent and can organise, summarise, and adapt the content.

This workflow also addresses one of the main frustrations that hearing-impaired students experience: the feeling of always being one step behind. During the lesson, the student can focus on understanding what they can hear and see, knowing that the full content is being captured. After the lesson, they can review and process the material at their own pace, filling in gaps and clarifying points that were unclear during the live session. This separation of capture and processing reduces the cognitive load during the lesson itself and allows the student to engage more fully with the classroom experience.

For teachers, this means that the hearing-impaired student is not constantly trying to take notes while simultaneously trying to hear and lip-read. Instead, the student can direct their attention to the lesson, participate in discussions to the extent possible, and deal with the written record afterwards. This shift in cognitive strategy can significantly reduce the fatigue and frustration that many hearing-impaired students experience in conventional classroom settings.

## Class Discussions and Group Work

One area where hearing-impaired students face particular difficulty is class discussions and group work. Multiple speakers, rapid exchanges, and overlapping conversation create an auditory environment that is challenging even for students with mild hearing loss. Traditional accommodations struggle in these settings because interpreters cannot easily follow rapid multi-speaker conversations, and note-takers may miss contributions or attribute them incorrectly.

AI transcription tools handle multi-speaker environments with varying degrees of success. Some tools can identify and label different speakers, which helps the student follow who said what. Others produce a single stream of text that captures the words but not the speaker attribution. In either case, the transcript provides more access than the student would have through hearing alone.

Teachers can support hearing-impaired students in discussion settings by establishing turn-taking norms (one person speaks at a time), repeating or summarising key points from student contributions, and ensuring that important conclusions or decisions from group work are stated clearly and written down. These practices benefit the transcription tool's accuracy and, more importantly, benefit the hearing-impaired student's ability to follow and participate in the conversation.

> **Warning**: Real-time transcription in noisy, multi-speaker environments is significantly less accurate than transcription of a single clear speaker. Teachers should not assume that the transcription tool captures everything perfectly during class discussions. It is important to check in with the hearing-impaired student after discussions and to provide written summaries of key points when the discussion involved important content.

## Privacy and Practical Considerations

Recording lessons for transcription raises important questions about privacy and consent. In many European jurisdictions, recording in educational settings is subject to data protection regulations. Schools should establish clear policies about when and how recording for accessibility purposes is permitted, who has access to the recordings, and how long they are retained.

A practical approach is to distinguish between the recording and the transcript. The audio recording may need to be deleted after the transcript is generated, while the transcript — which contains only text and no voice data — may be retained as a study resource. This approach preserves the accessibility benefit while minimising privacy concerns.

Students should also understand that transcripts are personal study tools, not documents to be shared widely. A transcript of a lesson might contain sensitive discussions, incorrect statements that were later corrected, or informal language that the teacher would not want circulated. Clear expectations about the appropriate use of transcripts help maintain trust between the student, the teacher, and the class.

## What This Means for Your Classroom

Supporting a hearing-impaired student with AI transcription requires minimal technical setup but benefits greatly from thoughtful integration. Allow the student to use a transcription tool during lessons. Adjust your speaking habits slightly — clear speech, moderate pace, facing the class — which benefits all students, not just those with hearing impairments. Repeat student questions before answering them, so the full exchange is captured in the transcript. And periodically review the student's processed transcripts to ensure they accurately reflect the lesson content.

You do not need to change your teaching fundamentally. The transcription tool runs quietly in the background. The AI processing happens after the lesson, on the student's own time. Your role is to ensure the conditions for accurate transcription and to verify that the student is using the tools effectively. For many hearing-impaired students, this combination of real-time capture and AI-assisted processing represents the most practical and effective accessibility tool currently available.

## Summary

- Real-time AI transcription converts the teacher's spoken content into written text as the lesson unfolds, giving hearing-impaired students access to the full content of the lesson in a reviewable, permanent format.
- Raw transcripts are valuable but unstructured — large language models can transform them into summarised key points, organised notes, vocabulary glossaries, and simplified explanations that serve as high-quality study materials.
- The most effective workflow combines real-time transcription during the lesson with AI-powered processing after the lesson, separating content capture from content organisation and reducing cognitive load during class.
- Teachers can significantly improve transcription accuracy through clear speech, moderate pace, facing the class while speaking, and repeating student questions before answering — adjustments that benefit all students.
- Class discussions and multi-speaker environments present the greatest challenge for transcription accuracy, and teachers should supplement AI tools with written summaries and clear turn-taking norms in these settings.
- Privacy considerations require clear school policies about recording, transcript retention, and appropriate use — distinguishing between temporary audio recordings and permanent text transcripts can help balance accessibility needs with data protection.
- AI transcription does not replace sign language interpreters, hearing aids, or other professional support — it provides a complementary tool that students can use independently, particularly in situations where traditional accommodations are unavailable.
