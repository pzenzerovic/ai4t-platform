## Language as an Accessibility Barrier

Across Europe, classrooms are becoming increasingly multilingual. Migration, mobility programmes, and the growing diversity of student populations mean that many teachers now work with students who do not speak the language of instruction fluently — or, in some cases, at all. For these students, every lesson is an accessibility challenge. The content may be perfectly appropriate for their age and ability level, but it is locked behind a language they have not yet mastered.

This is not a problem confined to specialist language classes or integration programmes. It affects mainstream mathematics lessons where a student cannot understand the problem statement, science classes where instructions for an experiment are incomprehensible, history lessons where the reading passage might as well be in code, and even physical education classes where verbal instructions leave the student standing still while classmates begin the activity. Language barriers cut across every subject and every moment of the school day.

Traditional responses to linguistic diversity in classrooms include bilingual teaching assistants, translated materials, and language support programmes that run alongside or outside regular classes. These are valuable and necessary. But they share the same limitation that other accessibility supports face: they require advance planning, additional resources, and often cannot cover every moment, every subject, and every spontaneous interaction that occurs during a school day.

AI translation and language adaptation tools offer a complementary approach that can operate in real time, across any subject, and without advance preparation. They do not replace language learning — a point that deserves emphasis — but they enable the student to access subject content while the process of learning the language of instruction is still underway.

> **Key Insight**: A student who does not yet speak the language of instruction fluently should not be locked out of subject content while they learn the language. AI translation tools allow students to access learning material in their subject areas immediately, without waiting months or years for language proficiency to develop.

## What AI Translation Can Do

Modern AI translation goes far beyond the word-by-word substitution of earlier machine translation systems. Large language models understand context, nuance, and register, producing translations that are natural and readable in the target language. More importantly for educational settings, AI can translate while preserving specific elements that the teacher wants to keep — such as technical terminology, proper nouns, or specific phrases that the student should learn in the language of instruction.

The simplest application is direct translation. A student receives a worksheet, a reading passage, or a set of instructions that they cannot understand. They paste the text into an AI tool and request a translation into their mother tongue. Within seconds, they have a version they can read and understand. This is functionally equivalent to having a bilingual assistant available at all times — except that the AI is available for every student, in every language, in every subject, without scheduling or cost.

But AI can do more than translate. It can adapt the language level of content, which is particularly valuable for students at different stages of language acquisition. A student who understands basic conversational language but struggles with academic text can ask the AI to explain the content using simpler vocabulary: "Explain this text at a B1 level of English" or "Rewrite this using only common, everyday words." The AI produces a version that is linguistically accessible while still conveying the academic content.

For students who are more advanced in the language of instruction, AI can serve as a bridge between their current level and the target level. Instead of fully translating the content, the student might ask the AI to explain only the words or phrases they do not understand: "Highlight the difficult vocabulary in this text and provide definitions in simple language." This approach supports continued language development by keeping the student engaged with the original text while removing the specific barriers that block comprehension.

## Voice Mode for Language Accessibility

AI voice interaction, discussed in the lesson on visual impairments, has a distinct and powerful application for multilingual students. In voice mode, a student can speak to the AI in their own language and receive responses in the language of instruction — or vice versa. This creates a real-time interpretation capability that can be used during lessons, homework, or independent study.

Consider a student who has recently arrived from Ukraine and is attending school in Croatia. The teacher gives verbal instructions for a group activity. The student opens their phone, activates voice mode, and whispers the instructions to the AI in Ukrainian: "The teacher said something about working in groups and making a poster. Can you explain what I need to do?" The AI responds with a clear explanation, either in Ukrainian or in simplified Croatian, depending on what the student finds most helpful.

This kind of interaction serves a dual purpose. It provides immediate access to the content of the instruction — the student knows what they need to do and can participate in the activity. And it provides incidental language learning, because the student is hearing and reading the language of instruction in a context where they already understand the meaning. Research on second language acquisition consistently shows that comprehensible input — language that the learner can understand because of contextual clues — is one of the most effective drivers of language development. AI-mediated translation creates exactly this kind of comprehensible input.

> **Tip**: Encourage multilingual students to use AI translation as a bridge, not a replacement. A useful workflow is to first read the original text in the language of instruction and identify what they understand, then use AI to translate or explain only the parts they do not understand. This keeps them engaged with the target language while ensuring they do not miss essential content.

## Preserving Technical Vocabulary Across Languages

One of the subtleties of translation in educational settings is the handling of technical vocabulary. In many subjects — science, mathematics, music, technology — technical terms are either the same across languages or have specific equivalents that students need to learn. A translation that replaces "photosynthesis" with its equivalent in the student's mother tongue might help with immediate comprehension, but it does not help the student learn the term they will encounter on the test, in the textbook, and in future lessons.

AI can handle this nuance effectively when prompted correctly. A teacher or student can instruct the AI: "Translate this text into Arabic, but keep all scientific terms in English." The result is a bilingual text that the student can understand in their mother tongue while simultaneously learning the technical vocabulary of the subject in the language of instruction.

This approach is particularly effective when combined with a glossary. The student can ask the AI to create a side-by-side vocabulary list: "Create a table with the scientific terms from this text in English in one column and their Arabic equivalents in the other column." The resulting glossary serves as a study tool that supports both subject learning and language development.

Teachers can prepare these bilingual glossaries in advance for key topics and share them with multilingual students before the lesson. With AI, creating such a glossary takes minutes rather than the hours it would take to research and compile manually. This is one of the areas where AI preparation time is dramatically shorter than traditional preparation time, making it practical for teachers to provide this kind of support routinely rather than only for major assessments.

## Cultural and Contextual Sensitivity

Translation is never purely linguistic — it is always cultural. An example that makes perfect sense in one cultural context may be meaningless or confusing in another. AI translations generally handle linguistic accuracy well, but cultural adaptation requires human oversight.

A mathematics problem about "splitting a restaurant bill" may need to be reframed for a student from a culture where communal meals are not typically divided into individual payments. A history text that assumes familiarity with Western European events may need contextual explanation for a student from a different region. A literature excerpt that relies on cultural references, humour, or wordplay may lose its meaning entirely in translation.

Teachers should be aware that AI translation provides linguistic equivalence but not necessarily cultural equivalence. When subject content relies heavily on cultural context — which is more common than many teachers realise — a brief conversation with the student about the cultural background can be more valuable than the most accurate translation. AI can assist with this by providing cultural context when prompted: "Explain the cultural background of this text for a student who has recently arrived from Syria." But the teacher's human understanding and sensitivity remain the essential component.

> **Warning**: AI translation is highly capable for factual and academic content but less reliable for culturally embedded content, idiomatic expressions, and texts that rely on shared background knowledge. Teachers should review translated materials for content that might be culturally confusing or inappropriate, and should be prepared to provide additional context when necessary.

## Multilingual Classrooms as an Asset

It is worth shifting perspective from the challenges of multilingual classrooms to their opportunities. Students who speak multiple languages bring cognitive advantages — enhanced executive function, metalinguistic awareness, and cultural knowledge — that benefit the entire class when they are recognised and valued.

AI tools can support this asset-based perspective in several ways. Multilingual students can serve as experts on their own languages, using AI to create bilingual resources that benefit other students who are curious about different languages. They can compare how concepts are expressed across languages, which deepens understanding for everyone. And they can use AI to create materials that celebrate their linguistic heritage while developing their skills in the language of instruction.

A teacher who says "Use AI to translate this into your language so you can understand it" is providing accessibility. A teacher who says "Use AI to show us how this concept is expressed in your language, and tell us what is interesting about the difference" is providing both accessibility and enrichment. The second approach affirms the student's identity, positions their multilingualism as a strength, and creates a more inclusive classroom culture that benefits all students.

## What This Means for Your Classroom

If you have students who do not speak the language of instruction fluently, AI translation tools can be integrated into your practice with minimal disruption. Allow students to use AI tools to translate worksheets, instructions, and reading materials during lessons. Encourage them to use the bridge approach — engaging with the original text first, then translating only what they cannot understand. Provide pre-made prompts for common translation needs: "Translate this text into [language] but keep the technical terms in [language of instruction]."

Over time, as the student's language proficiency develops, the extent of AI translation should decrease. The goal is gradual independence — from full translation to partial translation to vocabulary support to independent comprehension. AI makes it possible to calibrate this progression precisely, providing exactly the level of support the student needs at each stage of their language development.

The most important message for multilingual students is that their inability to understand the language of instruction is not a reflection of their intelligence or ability. It is a temporary barrier that AI can help them navigate while they develop the language skills that will eventually make the support unnecessary. This message — that the barrier is linguistic, not intellectual — is as important as the technology itself.

## Summary

- Language barriers in multilingual classrooms affect every subject and every moment of the school day, preventing students from accessing content that is appropriate for their age and ability level.
- AI translation tools provide near-instant translation of worksheets, instructions, and reading materials into virtually any language, functioning as an always-available bilingual assistant across every subject.
- Beyond direct translation, AI can adapt the language level of content to match the student's proficiency stage — from full translation for beginners to vocabulary support for more advanced learners — enabling a gradual path toward independent comprehension.
- Voice mode enables real-time spoken interaction in the student's mother tongue, providing immediate access to lesson content and creating comprehensible input that supports second language acquisition.
- Technical vocabulary handling is critical in educational translation — AI can be prompted to preserve subject-specific terms in the language of instruction while translating the surrounding text, supporting both content learning and language development.
- Cultural and contextual sensitivity requires human oversight, as AI provides linguistic equivalence but not necessarily cultural equivalence — teachers should review translations for culturally embedded content and provide additional context when needed.
- Multilingual students bring cognitive and cultural assets to the classroom, and AI tools can support an asset-based approach that positions their multilingualism as a strength rather than a deficit.
