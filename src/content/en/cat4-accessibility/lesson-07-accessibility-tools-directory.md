## A Practical Reference for Teachers

The previous lessons in this category explored how AI can support students with visual impairments, hearing impairments, cognitive processing difficulties, language barriers, and intellectual disabilities. Each lesson focused on principles and pedagogical approaches. This lesson takes a different form: it is a practical reference guide to the specific tools that can be used to implement those approaches in real classroom and mentoring settings.

This directory is not exhaustive. The landscape of AI and digital accessibility tools evolves rapidly, and new tools appear regularly while existing ones update their features. Rather than attempting to catalogue every available tool, this lesson presents a curated selection of tools that are currently available, practically useful in educational settings, and accessible to teachers without specialised technical knowledge. Each tool is described in terms of what it does, which students it helps, what role the teacher or mentor plays, and what practical considerations apply.

The tools are organised into three categories: general-purpose AI tools that serve multiple accessibility needs, specialised accessibility tools designed for specific impairments or difficulties, and supporting tools that complement AI-based approaches. For each tool, the focus is on practical application rather than technical specification — you do not need to understand how these tools work internally to use them effectively in your classroom.

> **Key Insight**: No single tool addresses all accessibility needs. Effective accessibility support typically involves combining a general-purpose AI tool (for text adaptation, translation, and explanation) with one or two specialised tools matched to the student's specific needs. The teacher's role is to select the right combination and integrate it into the learning process.

## General-Purpose AI Tools

General-purpose AI tools — large language models accessed through chat interfaces — are the most versatile accessibility tools available to teachers. They can simplify text, translate content, generate structured notes, explain concepts at different levels, and create learning materials adapted to individual needs. Their flexibility makes them useful across all four areas of accessibility covered in this category.

### ChatGPT

ChatGPT, developed by OpenAI, is the most widely known general-purpose AI tool. For accessibility purposes, its most relevant features are Vision mode, which allows image analysis through photographs, and Voice mode, which enables spoken interaction without requiring the student to read or type. ChatGPT can simplify complex texts, translate between languages, generate step-by-step instructions, create summaries and flashcards, and answer follow-up questions in a conversational format. It is accessible through web browsers and mobile applications on both iOS and Android.

For students with visual impairments, ChatGPT's Vision mode can analyse photographs of boards, textbooks, and worksheets, providing detailed descriptions and structured notes from visual content. For students with hearing impairments, it can process transcripts into organised study materials. For cognitive accessibility, it can simplify any text to a specified level. And for multilingual students, it can translate while preserving technical vocabulary. The free tier provides access to core features, though some advanced capabilities require a paid subscription.

### Claude

Claude, developed by Anthropic, offers similar general-purpose capabilities with a particular strength in longer, more nuanced text processing. Its Artifacts feature can generate interactive learning materials — such as flashcard applications or quiz interfaces — directly in the browser, which is especially useful for creating personalised practice materials adapted to a student's specific needs. Claude is accessible through web browsers and mobile applications.

For accessibility, Claude is particularly effective at detailed text simplification, creating structured study guides, and generating practice materials at calibrated difficulty levels. Its ability to create interactive Artifacts means that a teacher can generate an adapted learning activity — such as a simplified vocabulary quiz or a step-by-step guided exercise — without any programming knowledge.

### Microsoft Copilot

Microsoft Copilot, integrated into Microsoft 365 applications, offers AI assistance within the tools that many schools already use. For accessibility, its most relevant feature is integration with Microsoft Immersive Reader and other accessibility features built into Word, OneNote, and Teams. Copilot can simplify text, summarise documents, and generate adapted materials directly within the Microsoft ecosystem, which reduces the need to copy content between different applications.

## Specialised Accessibility Tools

While general-purpose AI tools are remarkably versatile, specialised tools are designed to address specific accessibility needs with greater depth and precision. These tools are worth knowing about even if you rely primarily on general-purpose AI, because they often provide features that general-purpose tools cannot.

### Transcription and Speech-to-Text

For students with hearing impairments, dedicated transcription tools provide more reliable and continuous speech-to-text conversion than general-purpose AI tools. Otter.ai is designed for real-time meeting and lecture transcription, offering features such as speaker identification, keyword highlighting, and searchable transcripts. It records audio and generates a running transcript simultaneously, which the student can review in real time or after the lesson. The transcripts can then be processed with a general-purpose AI tool for summarisation and restructuring.

Built-in transcription features in platforms like Microsoft Teams and Google Meet also provide real-time captions during video calls and virtual lessons, which is particularly relevant for hybrid or remote learning settings.

### Reading Support

Microsoft Immersive Reader, available in Word, OneNote, Teams, and Edge, provides a suite of reading support features designed for students with dyslexia and other reading difficulties. It can read text aloud with word-by-word highlighting, adjust text spacing and font size, break words into syllables, and highlight parts of speech. These features support decoding and comprehension without requiring any AI prompting — the student simply activates the features they need.

Read&Write by Texthelp provides similar reading and writing support with additional features for text prediction, vocabulary support, and screen masking that helps students with attention difficulties focus on one line of text at a time. It integrates with web browsers and common educational platforms.

### Task Decomposition

Goblin Tools is a specialised application designed to break complex tasks into smaller, manageable steps — a function that is particularly valuable for students with ADHD, executive function difficulties, or intellectual disabilities. The teacher or student enters a task description, and the tool generates a detailed step-by-step breakdown that can be used as a checklist. While general-purpose AI tools can perform this function when prompted, Goblin Tools is designed specifically for it and produces consistently well-structured results without requiring prompt engineering skills.

### Visual and Symbolic Communication

ARASAAC (Aragonese Portal of Augmentative and Alternative Communication) provides a free, open library of pictograms and symbols designed for communication support. For students with intellectual disabilities or severe language difficulties, pictogram-based instructions can be more accessible than any text, however simplified. ARASAAC symbols can be used to create visual schedules, task sequences, and communication boards that complement AI-adapted text materials.

Choiceworks is a visual support tool designed to help students understand daily routines, manage transitions, and make choices through picture-based sequences. It is particularly useful for students with autism spectrum conditions or intellectual disabilities who benefit from visual predictability and structured routines.

### Design and Visual Creation

Canva, covered in detail in Category 2 of this course, has significant accessibility applications. Teachers can use Canva to create visually simplified materials — worksheets with clear layouts, picture-based instructions, and structured visual guides — that complement text-based AI adaptations. For students who process visual information more effectively than text, a well-designed visual summary created in Canva can be more accessible than even the most carefully simplified paragraph.

> **Tip**: When selecting tools for a specific student, start with one general-purpose AI tool and one specialised tool matched to the student's primary need. Adding too many tools at once creates its own form of cognitive overload — for both the student and the teacher. Start simple, establish routines, and add additional tools only when the initial setup is working smoothly.

## Tool Selection by Student Need

The following overview maps common student needs to the tools best suited to address them. This is intended as a starting point for teachers who are unsure which tools to try first, not as a prescriptive recommendation. Every student's needs are different, and the best tool combination will vary depending on the individual, the subject, and the school context.

| Student Need | Primary Tool | Supporting Tool | Teacher's Role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cannot read the board clearly | ChatGPT Vision mode | Microsoft Immersive Reader | Allows phone use for photography; reviews AI descriptions |
| Cannot hear the teacher | Otter.ai (transcription) | ChatGPT or Claude (transcript processing) | Speaks clearly; repeats student questions; reviews transcripts |
| Struggles with complex text | ChatGPT or Claude (simplification) | Read&Write or Immersive Reader | Specifies appropriate simplification level; preserves key terms |
| Does not speak language of instruction | ChatGPT or Claude (translation) | Bilingual glossary (AI-generated) | Creates glossaries for key topics; encourages bridge approach |
| Has attention or focus difficulties | Goblin Tools (task decomposition) | ChatGPT (structured notes) | Provides checklist-based tasks; uses structured formats |
| Has intellectual disabilities | ChatGPT (mentor-guided simplification) | ARASAAC (pictograms), Choiceworks (routines) | Mediates all AI interactions; filters output; creates visual supports |

## Practical Considerations

Several practical considerations apply across all accessibility tools and should inform how teachers integrate them into their practice.

Cost and availability vary significantly. Some tools are free (ChatGPT basic tier, Claude free tier, ARASAAC, Microsoft Immersive Reader in existing Microsoft accounts), while others require paid subscriptions (Otter.ai premium features, Read&Write, ChatGPT Plus). Before recommending a tool to a student or family, verify that it is accessible without creating a financial burden.

Data privacy must be considered for every tool. Any tool that processes student work, records audio, or receives text about educational activities is handling data that may be subject to GDPR and national data protection regulations. Teachers should be aware of each tool's data handling practices and should avoid entering personal student information into any AI tool. The previous lesson on AI for students with intellectual disabilities discussed this in detail, and those principles apply to all accessibility tool use.

Device requirements vary. Most AI tools work on smartphones and tablets, which makes them accessible to most students. However, some specialised tools require specific operating systems or browser versions. Verify compatibility before introducing a tool into classroom practice.

Training and routine matter more than tool selection. A student who is confident with one tool and uses it routinely will get more benefit than a student who has access to many tools but uses none consistently. Invest time in establishing routines — especially for students with cognitive or intellectual disabilities, for whom predictability is itself an accessibility support.

> **Warning**: The tool landscape changes rapidly. A tool that is free today may require a subscription tomorrow. A tool that works well in one browser may not work in another. Periodically verify that the tools you recommend are still available, still free (if that was a factor in selection), and still functioning as expected. Building accessibility practices around a single tool that later becomes unavailable or unaffordable creates its own barrier.

## Summary

- General-purpose AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude are the most versatile accessibility tools available, capable of text simplification, translation, image analysis, voice interaction, and generation of adapted learning materials across all accessibility areas.
- Specialised tools address specific needs with greater depth: Otter.ai for transcription, Microsoft Immersive Reader and Read&Write for reading support, Goblin Tools for task decomposition, and ARASAAC for pictogram-based communication.
- Effective accessibility support typically combines one general-purpose AI tool with one or two specialised tools matched to the student's primary need — starting simple and adding tools gradually as routines are established.
- Tool selection should be guided by the student's specific needs, the subject context, and practical factors including cost, device compatibility, and data privacy requirements.
- Data privacy applies to all accessibility tools — teachers should understand each tool's data handling practices and should never enter personal student information into AI platforms.
- Training and routine are more important than tool selection — a student who uses one tool confidently and consistently will benefit more than a student with access to many tools but no established practice.
- The tool landscape evolves rapidly, and teachers should periodically verify that recommended tools remain available, affordable, and functional to ensure that accessibility practices remain sustainable.
