## Why Start with ChatGPT?

Of all the AI tools that have reached classrooms in the last few years, ChatGPT is the one most teachers encounter first. It is free to use in its basic form, requires no software installation, and can be accessed from any web browser or smartphone. For many teachers, the first serious contact with generative AI happens here — writing a question into a text box, reading the response, and beginning to form a sense of what this technology can and cannot do.

Because ChatGPT is often the entry point, how you begin shapes what you take away. A teacher who treats it as a curiosity and types one or two questions will form a shallow impression. A teacher who approaches it as a professional tool — with clear goals, realistic expectations, and an understanding of its limits — can extract real value from it within a single afternoon. This lesson is designed to help you do the latter. It walks you through setting up an account, understanding the interface, running your first useful workflows, and knowing where to be cautious.

The prompt engineering and meta-prompting skills from earlier lessons in this category apply directly here. What changes is the specific environment in which you use them.

## What ChatGPT Actually Is

ChatGPT is a *conversational AI assistant* built by the company *OpenAI* on top of a family of *large language models* — the same type of system explored in Category 1. When you type a question, the model predicts a sequence of words that forms a plausible, relevant response based on patterns it learned from enormous amounts of text during training. It is not searching a database of answers. It is generating new text in real time.

This matters because it explains both ChatGPT's strengths and its weaknesses. Its strengths are fluency, flexibility, and the ability to adapt to almost any topic or format. Its weaknesses are that it can produce confident-sounding information that is incorrect (a phenomenon called *hallucination*, covered in Category 1), that it has limited awareness of events after its training cutoff unless it is given the ability to browse the web, and that it does not remember past conversations unless you explicitly give it that context.

**Myth: "ChatGPT knows everything."** ChatGPT has absorbed patterns from a large portion of the public internet and many books and documents, which makes it appear knowledgeable across a vast range of topics. But it does not know whether what it produces is factually correct. It generates what sounds right given the conversation so far, which is often — but not always — the same as what is right. Teachers must verify factual claims, especially dates, statistics, quotations, and references.

## Setting Up an Account

To use ChatGPT, visit [chat.openai.com](https://chat.openai.com) and create a free account using an email address or a Google, Microsoft, or Apple login. The free tier gives you access to the standard model with reasonable usage limits — enough for most teachers to get substantial value without paying anything.

OpenAI also offers a paid tier called *ChatGPT Plus*, which provides access to more capable models, faster responses during busy periods, and additional features such as image generation and file uploads. Most teachers can start productively on the free tier and decide later whether the paid version is worth it for their workflow.

There are a few practical points worth knowing before you begin. The terms of service generally require users to be at least 13 years old, with parental consent for users under 18 in many jurisdictions. If you plan to have students interact with ChatGPT directly, you will need to consider school policy, parental consent, and the age requirements in your country. The safer starting point is to use ChatGPT yourself as a planning and preparation tool, and only later consider structured student use.

> **Tip**: Before using ChatGPT for any school-related task, check whether your school, district, or ministry of education has issued guidance on AI use. Many have published acceptable-use statements that affect what kinds of data you can and cannot enter into the tool. This is covered in more depth in the Considerations on AI category.

## The Interface at a Glance

When you open ChatGPT for the first time, the interface is deliberately simple. There is a sidebar on the left showing your past conversations, a large area in the centre where your current conversation appears, and a text box at the bottom where you type. That is essentially it.

Each new conversation starts fresh. ChatGPT does not automatically remember what you discussed in a previous chat unless you are using the optional *memory* feature or you paste relevant context into the new conversation. This is an important habit to build: if the AI needs to know something — your subject, your grade level, your curriculum, your teaching style — you must tell it at the start of the conversation.

At the top of the chat area you can usually select which model to use. The default free model is perfectly adequate for most teaching tasks. More capable models become available on paid tiers and are particularly useful for long or complex documents, careful reasoning, or tasks where the quality of the output matters a great deal.

## Your First Useful Workflows

Rather than asking ChatGPT impressive-sounding general questions, the fastest way to form a practical understanding of the tool is to run three or four workflows that produce something you can actually use tomorrow.

The first workflow is drafting a lesson starter. Open a new conversation and provide the context recommended in earlier lessons: your subject, grade level, the specific learning objective for the lesson, and any constraints such as lesson length or available materials. Then ask for a first-draft lesson outline. Read the result critically. You will almost certainly want to adjust the pacing, replace examples with ones that fit your students, and remove parts that do not serve your objective. The AI has done the blank-page work for you; your expertise turns the draft into a usable lesson.

The second workflow is creating differentiated materials. Paste a short text — a paragraph from a textbook, a news article, or a primary source — and ask ChatGPT to produce a version for a younger reading level, a version with simpler vocabulary for students learning the language of instruction, and a more challenging version with comprehension questions for advanced learners. Within a minute you have three versions of the same text suitable for a mixed-ability classroom.

The third workflow is generating practice questions. Provide a topic and ask for ten multiple-choice questions with correct answers and brief explanations, or five short-answer questions with a marking guide. Review the output for accuracy and classroom fit. This is often faster than writing questions from scratch, though you must read each question carefully before using it.

The fourth workflow is rewriting your own text. Paste a parent letter, a project brief, or a set of classroom instructions and ask ChatGPT to make the language clearer, shorter, or more appropriate for a specific audience. This is one of the most reliable uses of the tool, because you start with content you already know is correct.

> **Key Insight**: The most productive way to use ChatGPT as a teacher is not to ask it for finished products, but to use it for first drafts, alternative versions, and structured feedback on work you already have. Your professional judgement turns its output into something pedagogically sound.

## Safety, Privacy, and Student Data

Anything you type into ChatGPT is sent to OpenAI's servers and may be used to improve their models unless you opt out in the settings. This has direct implications for what teachers should and should not enter into the tool.

Do not paste student names, student work with identifying information, confidential school documents, parent contact details, or anything covered by data protection regulations such as the *GDPR* in the European Union. When you want help with something that would normally contain student data, anonymise it first — replace names with "Student A", "Student B", and remove any detail that could identify a real person or context.

Similarly, be cautious about pasting in large blocks of proprietary material — textbook chapters, copyrighted resources, or internal school documents. Use excerpts or summaries rather than complete works when you can, and respect the same professional norms you would apply to photocopying or sharing any other material.

> **Warning**: ChatGPT is a tool for you as a professional, not a system for handling student data. Treat it as you would treat an unsecured channel: everything you type leaves your device. If a piece of information would not be appropriate to post on a public forum, do not enter it into ChatGPT either.

## Moving from First Use to Regular Practice

The gap between trying ChatGPT once and integrating it into your weekly workflow is the gap where most teachers lose momentum. Bridging it requires two deliberate choices.

The first choice is to identify a small number of recurring tasks in your week that the tool is well suited to. Common candidates include drafting quiz questions, producing differentiated reading passages, summarising long documents for your own preparation, brainstorming discussion questions, and rewriting materials for different audiences. Pick two or three of these and commit to using ChatGPT for them consistently for a couple of weeks. You will develop a realistic intuition for where it helps, where it adds friction, and where it produces more work than it saves.

The second choice is to build a small library of your own effective prompts. Every time you write a prompt that produces a genuinely useful result, save it somewhere — a document, a note, or a dedicated file — with a short description of when it worked. Over time this library becomes your personalised toolkit. The next time you need ten multiple-choice questions on a new topic, you do not start from scratch; you adapt the prompt that worked last week. This habit alone compounds into significant time savings over a school year.

The goal is not to become an AI expert. The goal is to become a teacher whose preparation time is spent where it matters most — on the thinking, the judgement, and the pedagogy that no tool can perform for you.

## Summary

- ChatGPT is a free, browser-based conversational AI built by OpenAI on top of large language models; it generates new text rather than retrieving stored answers, which explains both its fluency and its tendency to produce confident but incorrect information.
- Setting up an account takes only a few minutes, and the free tier is sufficient for most teacher workflows; paid tiers add more capable models and features that become useful as your use deepens.
- Each conversation starts fresh, so teachers must supply context — subject, grade level, learning objective, constraints — at the start of the conversation if the response is to be relevant.
- Four high-value starter workflows are drafting lesson outlines, producing differentiated versions of a text, generating practice questions, and rewriting your own material for clarity or audience fit.
- The most productive mental model is to treat ChatGPT as a first-draft generator and alternative-version producer whose output you review and refine, not as an oracle that produces finished classroom materials.
- Teachers must avoid entering student data, confidential school documents, or proprietary materials into the tool; everything typed leaves your device and may be used to train future models unless you opt out.
- The gap between first use and regular practice is bridged by identifying a small set of recurring tasks and building a personal library of prompts that have produced useful results.
