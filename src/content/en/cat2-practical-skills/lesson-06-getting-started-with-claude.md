## Why Another AI Assistant?

After working through the previous lesson on ChatGPT, a reasonable question is why a teacher would need to know about a second AI assistant at all. The short answer is that different AI systems have different strengths, different interface choices, and different approaches to safety and long-form work. The assistants look similar at first — a chat box, a send button, a response appearing line by line — but the experience of working with them is not identical, and in some classroom tasks one will suit you better than the other.

Claude is a conversational AI assistant built by the company *Anthropic*. Anthropic was founded by former researchers at OpenAI and has placed particular emphasis on designing AI systems that behave safely and predictably even in ambiguous situations. For a teacher, what matters is not the company's research agenda but the practical consequences: Claude tends to produce longer, more structured written work comfortably, handles large documents well, and is often chosen by users who work extensively with text — writing, summarising, editing, and analysing.

This lesson mirrors the structure of the previous one. It walks you through what Claude is, how to set up access, the features that matter most for teaching, a few practical starter workflows, and the same privacy caveats that apply to any AI assistant.

## What Claude Is

Like ChatGPT, Claude is built on a *large language model* — a system that learned patterns in written language from enormous amounts of text and uses those patterns to generate responses. From the user's perspective, both tools do the same core thing: you type something in, and they produce text in reply. The differences are in behaviour, not in fundamental mechanism.

Anthropic publishes several versions of Claude, usually organised into a family of models with different capability and speed trade-offs. The more capable models produce more careful and structured writing; the faster models respond more quickly and are suitable for simpler tasks. As a teacher, you do not need to track the model names. You need to know that the version offered on the free tier of the website is generally sufficient for most preparation work, and that higher tiers unlock more capable models and additional features.

Claude is accessed primarily through [claude.ai](https://claude.ai), where you can create an account and begin typing immediately. There is also a mobile application for the most common platforms. The interface follows the same basic pattern as ChatGPT: a list of past conversations on the left, the current conversation in the centre, and a text box at the bottom.

**Myth: "Claude and ChatGPT are basically the same tool."** They share the same underlying technology category, but the outputs differ in ways that become noticeable when you use them on the same task. Claude tends to produce more continuous prose, is often more willing to refuse borderline requests, and has a different sense of what counts as a well-structured answer. The practical advice is to try both on the same teaching task and pick whichever produces the kind of draft you prefer to edit.

## Setting Up Access

To start using Claude, visit [claude.ai](https://claude.ai) and sign up with an email address or one of the common single sign-on options. The free tier provides meaningful access with usage limits that reset on a regular schedule. Paid tiers — typically branded *Claude Pro* or similar — provide higher limits, earlier access to the most capable models, and additional features such as projects and artefacts.

As with ChatGPT, the platform has age requirements stated in its terms of service, and teachers considering direct student use should check both the terms and any guidance published by their school or ministry of education. The safer default is to use Claude yourself for preparation and to introduce structured student use only once you have a clear view of both the tool and the applicable policies.

> **Tip**: A productive habit is to keep one AI assistant tab open as part of your preparation workspace — the same way you might keep your gradebook or lesson planner open. Making it visible in your workflow is the simplest way to ensure that you actually use it when it would save time, rather than forgetting that it exists.

## Features That Matter for Teaching

Several Claude features are particularly valuable for teachers, and they are worth knowing about before you begin.

The first is its handling of long documents. Claude is designed to work with substantial pieces of text in a single conversation — you can paste in a chapter, a curriculum document, or a long reading passage and ask for a summary, a differentiated version, or a set of comprehension questions in one step. This makes it well suited to the type of preparation work teachers do when starting a new unit and need to process a large amount of source material quickly.

The second is the *Projects* feature available on paid tiers. A project is a persistent workspace that can hold reference documents — your curriculum, your style guide, examples of your preferred lesson format — and make them available to every conversation within that project. This solves the "fresh conversation" problem described in the previous lesson. Once you have uploaded your curriculum and a couple of example lesson plans to a project, every new conversation in that project starts with that context already available, without you needing to paste it in each time.

The third is *Artefacts*, a feature that allows Claude to produce certain types of content — long written documents, structured lists, simple interactive materials — in a dedicated panel that you can edit and refine. For a teacher producing a worksheet or a structured lesson plan, this keeps the draft visible alongside the conversation where you are refining it.

> **Key Insight**: Where ChatGPT often feels like a quick-question tool, Claude often feels more like a long-form collaborator. If your week involves a lot of writing, document work, and sustained preparation tasks, you may find that Claude fits that kind of work naturally. This is a generalisation, not a rule — the best way to find out is to use each on real tasks from your own week.

## Your First Useful Workflows

The quickest way to form a practical understanding of Claude is, as with any new tool, to run a handful of realistic workflows.

A first workflow is processing a long reading. Paste in a chapter, an article, or a source text, and ask Claude to produce a concise summary, a list of the main arguments, a set of comprehension questions at three levels of difficulty, and a short writing prompt that asks students to respond to one of the ideas. Instead of performing four separate tasks, you get a structured package of materials from a single conversation.

A second workflow is drafting a unit plan. Describe the topic, the age group, the available lesson time, the learning objectives, and any constraints, and ask Claude to propose a sequence of lessons with clear objectives and a short description of each. Read it critically, move lessons around, and ask Claude to adjust the sequence until it reflects what you want. This is the kind of sustained, iterative task where Claude's conversational style tends to work well.

A third workflow is drafting a rubric. Describe the assessment task, the learning objectives, the age group, and the performance levels you want the rubric to distinguish. Ask Claude to propose a rubric with clear criteria and descriptive performance indicators at each level. Claude is generally comfortable producing structured assessment materials of this kind, and the result provides a solid first draft that you can tighten.

A fourth workflow is using a project as a teaching workspace. If you have access to the Projects feature, create one project per course you teach. Upload the curriculum, any style guides, and a few examples of your preferred lesson format. From then on, every conversation in that project already knows the context you would otherwise have to paste in. Over a term this compounds into a significant time saving.

## Safety, Privacy, and Student Data

The privacy principles from the previous lesson apply here without modification. Anything you type or upload is sent to Anthropic's servers and processed by their systems. The specifics of what is retained, for how long, and under what conditions should be checked directly in Anthropic's current privacy documentation, because these policies evolve over time.

In practical terms, the rules are the same as for any AI assistant. Do not paste student names, identifying details, confidential school documents, or material covered by data protection regulations such as the *GDPR*. Anonymise student work before pasting it — replace names with generic placeholders and strip anything that could identify a real person or context. Treat anything you type as information that has left your device, and act accordingly.

> **Warning**: The fact that one AI assistant has a slightly different privacy posture than another does not change the fundamental principle. Student data belongs inside the systems your school has approved for handling it. AI assistants are not those systems. Use AI for anonymised or non-personal material, and use your school's established systems for anything involving real student information.

## Using Claude Alongside Other Tools

Teachers often ask whether they should choose one AI assistant and stay with it, or use several. There is no single correct answer, but a simple approach works well in practice.

Start by using a single tool — whichever one you have easiest access to — and develop a working familiarity with it. Once you can routinely get useful results from that tool on three or four recurring teaching tasks, try a second one on the same tasks. You will quickly notice which feels better for which kinds of work. Many teachers settle into using different tools for different purposes: one for quick questions and fast drafts, another for long-form work and sustained projects.

This is similar to the way teachers already use different classroom tools for different purposes — a whiteboard for spontaneous explanation, a document projector for shared reading, a prepared slideshow for structured presentation. Each tool is best at a particular kind of task. AI assistants are the same. You do not need to choose one and abandon the others; you need to develop a sense of which tool suits which kind of preparation work.

The underlying skill remains constant across all of them. Clear context, well-structured prompts, and a willingness to treat AI output as a first draft to be refined by your professional judgement — these are the habits that produce good results regardless of which assistant you happen to be using on a given day.
