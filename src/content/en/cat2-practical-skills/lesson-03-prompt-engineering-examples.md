## From Principles to Practice

The previous lesson introduced the fundamental principles of prompt engineering: defining goals, specifying audiences, constraining scope, assigning roles, setting expectations, providing examples, and requesting step-by-step reasoning. Understanding these principles is important, but what ultimately matters is being able to apply them in practice.

This lesson presents a series of detailed, subject-specific examples that show how these principles work in real teaching scenarios. Each example follows a consistent pattern: a teaching task is identified, a well-crafted prompt is presented, and the reasoning behind the prompt is explained — highlighting which principles are in play and why they produce better results than a simpler, less structured request.

The examples are drawn from a range of subjects and grade levels precisely because the principles are universal. Whether you teach mathematics, science, languages, history, or literature, the same core practices apply. As you read, pay attention not to the specific subject matter but to the patterns of how the prompts are constructed. Those patterns are what you will carry into your own practice.

## Example 1: Mathematics — Generating Practice Exercises

**The task**: Create a set of addition exercises for Year 1 students practising sums up to 10.

**A simple prompt**: "Make maths exercises for Year 1."

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a Year 1 mathematics teacher. Create a worksheet containing 20 addition exercises. Each exercise should involve adding two single-digit numbers (0 to 9) with a sum of 10 or less. Use each digit at least once across the set. Present the exercises without answers, with a blank line for students to write their responses. Include a header with space for the student's name and date."

The well-crafted prompt applies several principles simultaneously. It assigns a role (Year 1 maths teacher), defines the goal precisely (20 addition exercises), constrains the mathematical parameters (single-digit numbers, sum no greater than 10), and specifies the format (no answers, name field, date field). The instruction to use each digit at least once is a methodological constraint that ensures the exercises are pedagogically balanced rather than randomly generated.

> **Tip**: When generating mathematical exercises, always specify the exact operation, the number range, and any methodological requirements. Without these constraints, AI models tend to cluster around certain number combinations and may produce an unbalanced set.

After receiving the initial output, the iterative approach allows you to refine it further. You might follow up with "Now create a version with answer keys" or "Adjust the difficulty so that five of the exercises involve adding zero." Each refinement builds on the context of the original prompt.

## Example 2: Science — Structured Problem Sets

**The task**: Create physics problems on electrical resistance for Year 8 students.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a secondary school physics teacher. Create 5 word problems for Year 8 students on calculating the resistance of conductors. Use different conductor materials (copper, aluminium, iron, silver) and vary the parameters (length, cross-section). Include a reference table of material resistivities at the end. Present the problems without solutions."

This prompt demonstrates the power of combining role assignment with specific technical parameters. By naming the conductor materials and asking for varied parameters, the prompt ensures that students encounter a range of problem types rather than repetitive variations of the same calculation. The request for a reference table means the output is self-contained — students have everything they need to solve the problems without consulting additional resources.

The underlying pedagogical principle here is that the teacher has thought carefully about what a good problem set looks like before asking the AI to generate it. The AI handles the production work, but the pedagogical design remains firmly in the teacher's hands.

## Example 3: Language Learning — Story-Based Exercises

**The task**: Create a reading comprehension activity for Year 6 English students practising the Present Simple tense.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are an English teacher for Year 6 students. Write a short story of 8 to 10 sentences using the Present Simple tense, about a character's daily morning routine. Then create four exercises based on the story: (1) a True/False exercise with 6 questions, (2) a fill-in-the-gaps exercise with 6 sentences where students must write the correct verb form, (3) a question-formation exercise with 5 items where students construct questions from given words, and (4) a short writing task where students write 5 sentences about their own daily routine. Include answer keys for exercises 1 through 3."

This is a particularly effective prompt because it mirrors the way a language teacher would structure a lesson around a text. The story provides the context, and the exercises move from receptive skills (true/false, gap-fill) through productive skills (question formation) to free production (writing about themselves). The prompt does not just ask for "some exercises" — it specifies a coherent pedagogical sequence.

Notice also how the prompt requests answer keys for the objective exercises but not for the free writing task, which reflects the reality that open-ended writing cannot be reduced to a single correct answer.

## Example 4: Literature — Analytical Essay Preparation

**The task**: Create materials to guide students through writing an analytical essay.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a secondary school literature teacher. Create a worksheet for students who will write a 400-to-440-word analytical essay on the topic: 'What are the reasons for the protagonist's downfall?' The worksheet should include: the essay question with clear formatting requirements, structural guidance (introduction, thesis statement, body with two arguments, conclusion), explicit instructions on how to support an argument with textual evidence without retelling the plot, a concrete example of a good approach (claim, evidence, analysis) and a contrasting example of a poor approach (plot summary without analysis), a list of relevant literary terms students should use, and a self-assessment checklist."

This prompt is notable for its pedagogical sophistication. Rather than asking the AI to write the essay itself, it asks for scaffolding materials that help students write their own essay. The explicit instruction to show the difference between analysis and plot retelling addresses one of the most common problems in student literary essays. The self-assessment checklist empowers students to evaluate their own work before submission.

> **Key Insight**: Some of the most powerful uses of AI in education are not about generating content for students but about generating scaffolding that helps students produce better work themselves. Prompts that ask for rubrics, checklists, model examples, and structured guidance often have more pedagogical impact than prompts that ask for finished products.

## Example 5: History — Structured Knowledge Review

**The task**: Create a review resource on key inventions of the 19th and 20th centuries.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a secondary school history teacher. Create a chronological overview of the most important inventions from 1800 to 2000. For each invention, include the inventor's name, the year, the country, and a one-to-two-sentence description of the invention's significance. Sort by year. Include at least 12 inventions spanning different decades and covering transport, communication, medicine, and computing."

The constraints in this prompt serve a clear educational purpose. Requiring inventions across different domains (transport, communication, medicine, computing) ensures that students see technological progress as a multi-dimensional phenomenon rather than a narrative dominated by a single field. The chronological sorting makes patterns visible — students can observe how the pace of innovation accelerated over time.

## Example 6: Geography — Interactive Review Materials

**The task**: Create a comprehensive review activity on EU member states and their capitals.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a geography teacher for lower secondary students. Create a review resource about the 27 EU member states and their capitals. Organise the states by European region (Western, Northern, Southern, Central/Eastern). Then create four exercises: (1) a fill-in-the-blanks table where some countries and some capitals are missing, (2) a matching exercise where students connect 5 countries to their capitals, (3) a geography quiz with 5 questions about regional groupings and notable facts, and (4) a sorting exercise where students assign given countries to their correct region. Include answer keys for all exercises."

This example shows how a well-structured prompt can generate a complete, multi-activity review resource in a single interaction. The regional grouping is both a presentation choice and a pedagogical strategy — it gives students a spatial framework for organising what might otherwise be an overwhelming list of 27 country-capital pairs.

## Example 7: Lesson Planning

**The task**: Create a complete lesson plan for a literature class.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a secondary school literature teacher. Create a detailed lesson plan for a 45-minute class on the analysis of a key novel. The plan should include: the teaching topic and unit, the type of lesson, teaching methods, forms of work, required materials, learning outcomes aligned with the national curriculum, and a timed lesson structure (introduction 10 minutes, main activity 25 minutes, closing 10 minutes). In the main activity, students should analyse a problem question about the protagonist's motivation. Design activities that develop analytical thinking and avoid plot retelling."

This prompt produces output that matches the formal lesson plan structure that many school systems require. By specifying the time allocation and the pedagogical approach (analytical thinking, not retelling), the teacher maintains control over the lesson design while delegating the documentation work to the AI.

> **Tip**: When using AI to generate lesson plans, always specify the time structure and the pedagogical approach you want. Without these constraints, AI tends to produce generic plans that look complete on paper but lack the specific methodological choices that make a lesson effective.

## Example 8: Assessment — Rubrics and Grading Criteria

**The task**: Create assessment criteria and grade descriptors for a student essay.

**A well-crafted prompt**: "You are a secondary school language teacher. Students have written analytical essays of 400 to 440 words. Create assessment criteria with three components: literary analysis (10 points), written expression (10 points), and language accuracy (10 points), for a total of 30 points. Define grade boundaries with 60 percent as the pass threshold and 90 percent for the highest grade. For each grade level (1 through 5), write a complete descriptor that integrates all three components into a single statement, written in third person singular."

Assessment design is an area where AI can save teachers significant time without compromising pedagogical quality — provided the prompt specifies the assessment framework precisely. The request for integrated grade descriptors (rather than separate scores per component) reflects the actual format many teachers need for electronic gradebook entries.

## Patterns to Take Away

Across all of these examples, several patterns emerge that you can apply regardless of your subject or grade level. First, every effective prompt begins with a clear role and audience. Second, specific constraints on format, length, and structure consistently produce better results than open-ended requests. Third, the most useful prompts anticipate what the final product needs to look like — answer keys, student name fields, reference tables, self-assessment checklists — and include those requirements from the start. Fourth, the prompts reflect pedagogical thinking that happens before the AI interaction begins.

The AI is a powerful production tool, but it is not a pedagogical designer. The design comes from you. The principles you learned in the previous lesson and the patterns you have seen in these examples give you the vocabulary to translate your pedagogical intentions into prompts that produce useful, classroom-ready output.

## Summary

- Effective prompts combine multiple principles simultaneously — role assignment, audience specification, format constraints, and explicit output requirements work together to produce classroom-ready results.
- Mathematical and scientific prompts benefit from precise specification of operations, number ranges, units, and methodological requirements to ensure pedagogically balanced output.
- Language learning prompts are most effective when they mirror natural pedagogical sequences, moving from receptive exercises through guided production to free production.
- Some of the highest-impact uses of AI in education involve generating scaffolding materials — rubrics, checklists, model examples, and structured guidance — rather than finished student-facing content.
- Assessment-related prompts should specify the grading framework, point allocations, grade boundaries, and descriptor format to produce output that matches institutional requirements.
- The consistent pattern across all effective prompts is that the pedagogical design comes from the teacher; the AI handles the production work within the framework the teacher defines.
- These examples are starting points, not rigid templates — adapt them to your own subject, grade level, and institutional context using the underlying principles.
