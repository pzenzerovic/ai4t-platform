## Why a Purpose-Built Platform Matters

In the previous lessons, you learned how to use general-purpose AI tools like ChatGPT and Claude through effective prompting. Those tools are powerful and flexible, but they require you to write detailed prompts every time. MagicSchool.ai takes a different approach. Instead of a blank chat interface, it provides over 80 specialised tools — each designed for a specific teaching task — with guided forms that ask you for exactly the information the AI needs.

Think of the difference this way: using ChatGPT for lesson planning is like cooking with a well-stocked kitchen — you can make anything, but you need to know the recipe. Using MagicSchool is like having a recipe app that asks you "What dish? How many servings? Any dietary restrictions?" and then generates the recipe. Both approaches have their place, but MagicSchool is particularly effective for routine preparation tasks where the structure of the output is predictable and the variable is your specific classroom context.

This lesson walks you through the platform, its core workflow, and two practical examples — creating a differentiated lesson plan and generating a quiz from a YouTube video — so you can see how the tool fits into everyday teaching practice.

## Getting Started

To use MagicSchool, you need an account and a modern web browser. Visit [magicschool.ai](https://www.magicschool.ai) and sign up with your email or your existing Google or Microsoft account. The free tier gives you access to all 80+ tools with a limited number of generations per month.

![The MagicSchool Teacher Tools dashboard showing available AI tools organised by category](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-03-teacher-tools.png)

Every tool includes a "Show an Exemplar" button that displays a sample output, helping you understand what is possible before you start. This is a useful feature for first-time users — take a moment to review exemplars for the tools you plan to use most frequently.

> **Tip**: The MagicSchool interface is in English, but you can generate content in other languages — including Croatian, Greek, and Romanian — by specifying the language in the "Additional Context" field or by using the dedicated Translate tool, which supports over 90 languages.

## The Core Workflow

The MagicSchool workflow follows five steps that will feel familiar if you have been practising the prompt engineering principles from earlier lessons in this category.

In the first step, you choose a tool. Browse the categories or search by keyword. Each tool is designed for a specific task — Lesson Plan Generator, Worksheet Generator, Multiple Choice Quiz Maker, Rubric Generator, Text Leveller, and so on.

In the second step, you fill in the guided form. Each tool has specific fields: grade level, subject, topic, duration, learning objectives, and an "Additional Context" field where you can describe specific classroom needs. The more specific you are — especially in the Additional Context field — the better the output.

In the third step, you click Generate and wait a few seconds. MagicSchool produces a complete draft based on your inputs.

In the fourth step, you review and edit. Read through the output carefully. You can edit directly in MagicSchool, click "Regenerate" for a different version, or use *Raina* — the platform's AI chat assistant — to ask for specific modifications.

In the fifth step, you export. When satisfied, export to Google Docs, Microsoft Word, PDF, or copy directly to your learning management system. The free version supports copy-paste; paid versions offer one-click export.

## Example 1: Creating a Differentiated Lesson Plan

To see this workflow in practice, let us walk through creating a lesson plan using the Lesson Plan Generator. We selected this tool from the dashboard and filled in the form with specific details about the lesson.

![The Lesson Plan Generator form filled in with grade level, topic, learning objectives, and additional context about student needs](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-04-lesson-plan-form.png)

Notice the level of detail in the form. The topic field specifies not just "water cycle" but the exact learning objectives: students should be able to identify and explain the four main stages. The Additional Context field describes the specific classroom situation: a mixed-ability class with three English Language Learners and two students with reading accommodations, limited lab equipment, and a request for visual supports and hands-on activities.

This specificity is what makes the difference between generic output and something genuinely tailored to your classroom. You are applying the same prompt engineering principles you learned earlier — defining the goal, specifying the audience, and providing relevant context — but through a guided form rather than a blank text box.

After clicking Generate, MagicSchool produced a complete lesson plan within about ten seconds. The output included clearly stated objectives, a materials list that accounted for limited lab equipment, differentiation strategies for the ELL students and students with accommodations, and timed sections that fit within the 45-minute period.

![The generated lesson plan showing learning objectives, assessment, key points, and structured content](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-05-lesson-plan-output.png)

### Iterating with Raina

The initial lesson plan was good, but not perfect — which is exactly what you should expect. The hands-on activity (creating a water cycle diagram) might be too advanced for the ELL students without additional support. Rather than starting over, we clicked on Raina — the AI chat assistant in the left sidebar — and typed:

*"Can you modify the hands-on activity to include a simpler option for ELL students? Instead of labelling a diagram with written words, I would like them to have a cut-and-paste version where they match pictures to the correct stages."*

![Using Raina to request specific modifications to the generated lesson plan](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-06-raina-chatbot.png)

Raina immediately updated the lesson plan to include two versions of the activity: the original labelling task for most students, and a cut-and-paste matching activity for the ELL students. This kind of quick differentiation would have taken 15 to 20 minutes to create manually.

We noticed that the closing activity (an exit ticket) was text-heavy, so we asked Raina for another modification:

*"Change the exit ticket to a visual format — maybe a quick drawing where students show one stage of the water cycle they learned today, with just one sentence explanation."*

![Continuing the conversation with Raina to refine the exit ticket and add further differentiation details](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-07-raina-iteration.png)

> **Key Insight**: Raina transforms MagicSchool from a one-shot generator into an iterative conversation. Instead of regenerating the entire lesson plan when you want one change, you can ask Raina for specific modifications — just as you would ask a teaching assistant to adjust one part of a lesson while keeping the rest.

### Exporting the Result

Once satisfied with the lesson plan, we exported it using the export options at the top of the output panel.

![The export dialog showing options to print or export the generated content](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-08-export-dialog.png)

From the export dialog, you can send the lesson plan directly to Google Docs, download it as a Word document or PDF, or simply copy and paste it into your existing planning documents.

A useful follow-up workflow is to use other MagicSchool tools to create the supporting materials for the same lesson. After generating the lesson plan, you could use the Worksheet Generator to create the cut-and-paste activity for the ELL students, and the Multiple Choice Quiz Maker to create a formative assessment for the following day. This creates a coherent set of materials with aligned objectives and consistent language.

## Example 2: Creating a Quiz from a YouTube Video

One of MagicSchool's most practical tools for everyday teaching is the YouTube Video Questions generator. It takes any YouTube video URL and automatically creates comprehension questions based on the video content.

![The YouTube Video Questions tool form with grade level, question type, and video transcript fields](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-09-youtube-tool-form.png)

For this example, we wanted to create a follow-up quiz for an educational video about photosynthesis for Year 7 biology students. We selected the YouTube Video Questions tool, set the grade level and question type, and pasted the video transcript (MagicSchool provides instructions for obtaining YouTube transcripts).

MagicSchool analysed the transcript and generated comprehension questions that directly addressed the key concepts from the video, ranging from basic recall to application-level thinking.

![The generated quiz output showing multiple-choice questions with answer keys and timestamps](/images/lessons/practical-skills/magicschool-10-youtube-quiz-output.png)

The generated questions included answer keys with timestamp references back to the video. If any question was at the wrong difficulty level, Raina could simplify or elevate it with a single follow-up message.

This workflow — find a good video, generate questions, refine, export — takes less than five minutes. Creating the same quiz manually would take 20 to 30 minutes.

## Safety and Privacy

MagicSchool has strong privacy protections. The platform is FERPA, COPPA, and GDPR compliant, and has earned a 93 percent privacy rating from Common Sense Privacy — one of the highest ratings among AI platforms for education. MagicSchool explicitly states that it never sells user data and does not use personally identifiable information to train its AI models.

Despite these protections, the standard best practice applies: do not enter real student personal data when creating materials. Use placeholders like "Student A" or fictional names rather than real student names, specific diagnoses, or sensitive family information.

For schools using Enterprise accounts, MagicSchool provides additional controls: administrators can choose which tools are available to teachers, set up content moderation rules, and monitor usage across the organisation.

> **Warning**: Always review AI-generated content before using it with students. AI can produce factual errors, outdated information, or culturally inappropriate examples — particularly in science, history, and social studies content. You are the expert on your students and your context. Treat MagicSchool outputs as first drafts that need your professional review.

## Practical Guidelines

Several practices will help you get better results from MagicSchool. Use the Additional Context field generously — this is where you tell MagicSchool about your specific classroom: student needs, available materials, time constraints, and prior knowledge. The more context you provide, the more useful the output.

Combine multiple tools for a single lesson. Use the Lesson Plan Generator first, then use the Worksheet Generator, Quiz Maker, and Rubric Generator to create all the supporting materials. This creates a coherent unit with aligned assessments.

Use Raina for complex or nuanced requests. If the form-based tools do not give you exactly what you need, open Raina and describe your request in detail. Raina can handle more nuanced requests than the preset forms.

Always review for accuracy. Check dates, formulas, definitions, and any content that students will rely on. Your professional review is not optional — it is essential.
