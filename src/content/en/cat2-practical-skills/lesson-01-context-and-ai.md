## Why Context Is the First Thing You Need to Understand

If you have ever asked an AI tool a question and received a response that was technically correct but completely useless for your situation, you have already experienced the problem of context. The AI did not fail because it lacked knowledge. It failed because it lacked information about you, your students, and what you were actually trying to accomplish.

Context is the single most important concept in practical AI use, and it is also the most frequently misunderstood. Many teachers approach AI tools the way they might approach a search engine — type a quick question, expect a useful answer. But AI does not work like a search engine. It works like a conversation partner who knows an enormous amount about the world in general but knows absolutely nothing about your specific situation unless you tell it. Understanding this distinction is the foundation upon which every other skill in this category builds.

## What Exactly Is Context?

In AI terms, *context* refers to the complete set of information that is available to the AI at the moment it generates a response. This includes everything you have written in the current conversation: your questions, your follow-up instructions, any details you have provided about your goals, and the AI's own previous responses.

The critical thing to understand is that context is not the same as knowledge. An AI model like ChatGPT or Claude has been trained on vast amounts of text and has learned patterns across many domains. That is its general knowledge. But it has no persistent awareness of who you are, what subject you teach, how old your students are, or what you did in yesterday's lesson. If you do not provide that information explicitly, the AI treats it as if it does not exist.

> **Key Insight**: AI does not "know" anything about your specific situation. Every piece of relevant information — the age of your students, the subject you teach, the learning objectives you are targeting — must be provided by you within the conversation. This is context, and it determines the quality of every response you receive.

Think of it this way: if a colleague walked into the staffroom and said "Make me a worksheet," you would immediately ask a series of clarifying questions. What subject? What grade? What topic? How many questions? What format? You would ask because you understand that without this information, you cannot produce anything useful. AI cannot ask those questions on its own. It will simply do its best with whatever information it has — and if that information is thin, the result will be generic.

## Context as Working Memory

A useful analogy for understanding how context operates is the concept of *working memory*. Just as a person can hold a certain amount of information in mind during a conversation — but not an unlimited amount — AI operates within a defined context window that determines how much information it can process at once.

Modern AI models have remarkably large context windows. Some can process the equivalent of tens of thousands of words in a single conversation, which in practical terms means hundreds of pages of text. This is far more than most teachers will ever need in a single session. However, the context window is not infinite. When a conversation becomes very long, older parts of the exchange may effectively fall out of the model's working memory.

This is why you may occasionally notice that an AI tool seems to "forget" instructions you gave earlier in a long conversation. It is not a bug or a failure of the system. It is a technical limitation of the working memory. The information has moved beyond the boundary of what the model can actively process. For most educational tasks — generating a worksheet, planning a lesson, creating assessment questions — this limit is unlikely to be reached. But it is useful to understand why it exists, because it explains a pattern that many users find confusing.

> **Tip**: If you are working on a complex task that involves a long conversation, periodically restate your key requirements. This ensures that the most important information remains within the AI's active working memory, even as the conversation grows.

## Why Context Directly Determines Response Quality

The relationship between context quality and response quality is not subtle — it is direct and proportional. The richer and more relevant the context you provide, the more precise, useful, and classroom-ready the AI's output will be. When context is vague, fragmented, or contradictory, the responses will reflect exactly those qualities.

Consider a concrete example. A teacher who types "Create a quiz about fractions" will receive a generic quiz with no particular grade level, no specific learning objectives, and no alignment with what the class has actually covered. The same teacher who writes "Create a 10-question quiz on adding fractions with unlike denominators for Year 6 students who have just completed a two-week unit on this topic, including two word problems and an answer key" will receive something dramatically more useful. The difference is not in the AI's capability — it is in the context provided.

This principle mirrors something teachers already understand deeply from their own practice. When you give students a clear assignment brief with explicit expectations, rubrics, and examples, the quality of their work improves. When instructions are vague, students produce vague work. AI responds to clarity in exactly the same way.

## Context in Educational Practice

In an educational setting, the kinds of contextual information that make the biggest difference are often things you already think about when planning a lesson. They include the age and grade level of your students, their approximate level of prior knowledge, the specific learning objectives you are targeting, the format you want the output in, and — where relevant — the role you want the AI to adopt.

Each of these elements narrows the AI's response from a broad, generic answer toward something that is genuinely applicable in your classroom. The age of students affects vocabulary and complexity. Prior knowledge determines where the explanation should start. Learning objectives shape which aspects of a topic to emphasise. The desired format — a worksheet, a discussion prompt, an assessment rubric — determines the structure of the response. And role-setting, such as asking the AI to respond as a biology teacher for seventh graders, adjusts tone and approach in ways that are often surprisingly effective.

As you explored in Category 1, AI models generate responses by predicting the most likely continuation of the text they have been given. When you provide rich, specific context, you are effectively steering those predictions toward the kind of output you actually need. Without that steering, the AI defaults to the most statistically average response — which is rarely what a specific classroom situation requires.

## Common Mistakes With Context

Two mistakes appear with particular frequency among teachers who are beginning to work with AI tools. The first is the assumption that the AI will figure out what you mean. If your goal, audience, or constraints are not explicitly stated, the system has no reliable way to infer them. It may sometimes guess correctly, but it will just as often miss the mark. The solution is simple: state what you need, who it is for, and what the boundaries are.

The second common mistake is the opposite problem — overloading the context with irrelevant information. Although modern context windows are large, their effectiveness depends on the relevance of the information, not merely the quantity. Pasting an entire curriculum document when you only need a single lesson's objectives can actually reduce quality, because the AI has to determine which parts of that large input are relevant to your specific request. A focused, well-structured context — even if it is only a few sentences long — will often outperform a long, unfocused one.

> **Warning**: More text does not automatically mean better context. The AI responds best when the information you provide is relevant, clearly stated, and directly connected to what you are asking for. Think of context as a well-prepared lesson plan: concise, purposeful, and focused on the learning objectives.

## Context as the Foundation for Everything That Follows

Understanding context is not just one skill among many — it is the prerequisite for every other skill you will develop in this category. The lessons that follow on prompt engineering and meta-prompting are, at their core, techniques for providing better context. When you learn to write a well-structured prompt, you are learning to organise context in a way that guides the AI effectively. When you learn meta-prompting, you are learning to use dialogue with AI to build context collaboratively before committing to a specific task.

In this sense, context is to AI interaction what lesson planning is to teaching. A teacher who walks into a classroom without a plan can still teach — but the result will be inconsistent, unfocused, and often below the standard that the teacher is capable of achieving. The same is true for working with AI. You can always type a quick question and get a response. But if you invest a few moments in providing clear, relevant context, the quality of that response — and its usefulness in your actual classroom — improves dramatically.

The good news is that this is not a technical skill. You are not learning a programming language or memorising syntax. You are applying something you already do every day as a teacher: thinking clearly about what you want to achieve, who it is for, and what good output looks like. The only new element is that your audience is an AI system rather than a group of students — and the principles of clear communication apply just as strongly.

## Summary

- Context is the complete set of information available to an AI model at the moment it generates a response, and it is the single most important factor in determining output quality.
- AI models have vast general knowledge from training, but they have zero knowledge of your specific situation unless you provide it explicitly within the conversation.
- The context window functions like a large but finite working memory — in very long conversations, older information may fall outside the model's active processing range.
- The relationship between context quality and response quality is direct and proportional: specific, relevant context produces specific, useful output; vague context produces generic responses.
- The most valuable contextual information for educational tasks includes student age, prior knowledge level, learning objectives, desired output format, and the role the AI should adopt.
- Two common mistakes are assuming the AI will infer unstated requirements and overloading the context with irrelevant information — both reduce output quality.
- Context is the foundation for all other AI skills covered in this category, including prompt engineering and meta-prompting.
