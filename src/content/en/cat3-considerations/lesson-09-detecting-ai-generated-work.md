## Why Detection Is the Wrong Place to Start

Since the arrival of widely accessible generative AI, one of the most common questions from teachers has been how to tell whether a student's work was written by the student or by an AI. The question is understandable. Teachers are responsible for assessing what students have actually learned, and submissions that do not reflect the student's own thinking undermine that responsibility. It is natural to want a tool that can reliably separate genuine student work from AI-generated work.

The difficulty is that such a tool does not exist, and there are strong reasons to believe it never will. Several commercial products claim to detect AI-generated text, but independent research consistently finds that they produce both *false negatives* — missing AI-generated content — and *false positives* — flagging genuine student writing as AI-generated. The false positives are particularly serious: they can lead to accusations of dishonesty against students who have done nothing wrong, with real consequences for their academic record and well-being.

This lesson takes a different starting point. It explains why detection is unreliable, what teachers can and cannot infer from writing itself, and why the most durable response to AI misuse is not better surveillance but better assessment design. It builds on the ethics discussion from earlier in this category and the human-in-the-loop principle from the previous lesson.

## Why AI Detection Tools Fail

To understand why AI detectors are unreliable, it helps to understand what they actually measure. Most detection tools analyse statistical features of text — the predictability of word choices, the variation in sentence structure, the distribution of certain punctuation patterns — and compare them against patterns typical of human or AI writing. The underlying assumption is that AI-generated text has detectable statistical signatures that differ from human writing.

This assumption holds up only weakly. The text generated by modern AI models is itself trained on human writing and aims to produce text that is statistically indistinguishable from it. Each generation of models narrows the gap further. What is more, students can transform AI-generated text in ways that eliminate most of the detectable signatures — by asking the AI to write in a specific style, by editing the output, or by using a second AI tool specifically designed to rewrite text to avoid detection. The arms race between detection and evasion has a clear direction of travel, and detection is losing.

The false positive problem is equally important. Human writing that happens to be clear, well-structured, and grammatically conventional can trigger detectors. This disproportionately affects students who have worked hard to produce polished writing, students who write in a second language and use simpler vocabulary, and students who use writing support tools such as spell-checkers and grammar tools. The population most likely to be wrongly accused by AI detectors includes some of the students most likely to have produced their work honestly.

**Myth: "An AI detector with 99% accuracy is reliable enough to use in assessment."** Even a detector with 99% accuracy produces meaningful error rates at the scale of a school. If 1% of honest student submissions are flagged as AI-generated, then in a class of 100 students handing in two assignments per term, the detector will falsely accuse two students per term on average. Multiplied across a school, this amounts to dozens of wrongful accusations per year. No accuracy rate that is likely to be achieved in practice is high enough to make detection a fair basis for academic discipline.

> **Warning**: Using AI detection tools as the primary evidence for academic dishonesty puts students at serious risk of unjust accusation. Detection results can inform a conversation, but they cannot substitute for evidence of what actually happened. A detector flagging a submission is not proof of misconduct; it is, at most, a reason to look more carefully.

## What Teachers Can and Cannot Infer from Writing

There are some signs that an experienced teacher can notice in student work, though none of them are proof on their own. Sudden changes in a student's style, vocabulary, or structural sophistication from one submission to the next can be worth a conversation. Writing that uses technical terminology or references the student has not previously demonstrated familiarity with can also warrant attention. Submissions that answer a slightly different question from the one asked — or that are generic in ways that do not reflect classroom discussion — sometimes indicate AI-generated content pasted without editing.

But each of these signs has a completely innocent explanation. A student may have genuinely improved after extra study. A student may have read ahead. A student may have misread the question. An experienced teacher's intuition is a valid reason to look more carefully, but it is not, on its own, evidence. The temptation to move from "this looks unlike the student's previous work" to "this was written by AI" must be resisted.

The practical implication is that any response to suspected AI misuse must begin from the assumption that the teacher does not have definitive evidence. The appropriate response is a conversation — a non-accusatory discussion in which the student is asked to explain their work, walk through their thinking, and demonstrate understanding of what they submitted. If the student can do this, the work stands. If the student cannot, this itself is information about what they have and have not learned, independent of how the submission was produced.

## Shifting the Focus from Detection to Assessment Design

The most robust response to AI misuse is not to catch it after the fact but to design assessment in ways that make misuse less useful to the student. This is a shift in focus: away from surveillance and towards pedagogy.

Several principles guide this shift. The first is to assess the *process* as well as the *product*. A finished essay reveals only the final state of a student's work. A process-oriented assessment looks at the thinking that produced it: drafts, notes, intermediate versions, revisions in response to feedback. A student who has produced a polished final essay but cannot show any drafts, notes, or evidence of the thinking that led to it has produced something suspicious regardless of what any detector says — and a student who has genuinely worked through the task will have a natural trail of process evidence to point to.

The second principle is to include *in-class components* in significant assessments. A take-home essay may be supplemented by an in-class oral defence, a short written reflection produced in class, or a follow-up conversation in which the student explains their argument. The in-class component does not need to replace the take-home work; it needs only to confirm that the student can engage meaningfully with the work they submitted.

The third principle is to ask for *specific, contextual work*. A generic essay question — "Discuss the causes of World War One" — is relatively easy for an AI to answer well. A question that requires the student to connect the topic to specific material covered in class, specific discussions the class has had, specific local or personal examples, or specific sources the teacher has provided is much harder to answer convincingly without genuine engagement. AI can produce plausible general content; it struggles with content that depends on specific classroom context.

The fourth principle is to use *iterative feedback cycles*. Instead of a single high-stakes submission, break large tasks into stages with feedback at each stage. A student who is working with AI to produce the whole piece in one go will have difficulty engaging with stage-by-stage feedback in a coherent way. A student who has worked through the task themselves will engage with feedback naturally because they have a mental model of what they are trying to achieve.

> **Key Insight**: The assessments most vulnerable to AI misuse are exactly the assessments whose value was already in question: generic, take-home, one-shot submissions that measure the polish of a final product more than the thinking behind it. The arrival of AI is a forcing function for assessment design that reflects what we actually want to measure.

## Working with Students Rather Than Against Them

A detection-oriented response frames the teacher and the student as opponents. The teacher tries to catch misuse; the student tries to avoid being caught. This is exhausting for the teacher, demoralising for the student, and productive of neither trust nor learning. A better framing is one in which the teacher and student are working together on the question of what genuine learning looks like in a world where AI is available.

The first element of this approach is transparency. The classroom has explicit agreements about what AI use is and is not permitted for which tasks — often, the agreements will differ across tasks, with AI permitted for some (brainstorming, practice, reviewing drafts) and not for others (final submissions, assessments). Students know the rules and the reasons for them.

The second element is conversation about misuse without immediate assumption of dishonesty. If a submission does not seem to reflect the student's own thinking, the conversation is framed as an attempt to understand what happened, not as a prosecution. Students who misused AI will often acknowledge it when given the opportunity to explain without fear. Students who did not misuse AI can demonstrate their engagement with the work.

The third element is attention to the underlying reasons students misuse AI. A student who hands in AI-generated work on a task they could not complete may be signalling that the task was too hard, that they were too overloaded, or that they did not know where to start. These are teaching problems, not just disciplinary problems, and they have teaching responses — more scaffolding, clearer expectations, earlier check-ins, better support for students who are struggling. A classroom in which students feel able to ask for help rather than submit AI-generated work is a classroom with less AI misuse, not because students were prevented from cheating but because they did not need to.

## The Practical Programme

A teacher who wants to respond seriously to AI in student work has, in summary, a short practical programme. Do not rely on AI detection tools as evidence of misconduct. Be cautious about intuition as a basis for accusation and use conversation as the primary method for exploring suspicious work. Redesign assessment to include process evidence, in-class components, and context-specific questions that make generic AI responses less useful. Make the rules about permitted AI use explicit for each task, and explain the reasoning. Pay attention to the conditions that drive misuse, and address them. Treat the situation as a shared problem to be worked on with students, not a surveillance problem to be solved against them.

This programme does not eliminate AI misuse. Nothing does. But it reorients the classroom away from a detection game that teachers cannot win and towards an assessment culture that reflects what education is actually for: the development of understanding in real students, visible to their teachers, demonstrated through work that no shortcut can produce for them.

## Summary

- AI detection tools are unreliable in both directions — they miss AI-generated content and falsely flag genuine student work — and the gap between detection and evasion is widening, not closing.
- False positives disproportionately affect students who write clearly, students working in a second language, and students who use standard writing support tools, meaning detection carries a real risk of wrongful accusation.
- Human intuition about suspicious submissions is a legitimate reason to look more carefully, but it is not evidence of misconduct on its own; the appropriate response is a non-accusatory conversation, not a disciplinary decision.
- The most robust response to AI misuse is not detection but assessment design — assessing process alongside product, including in-class components, asking for context-specific work, and using iterative feedback cycles.
- Assessments that are most vulnerable to AI are exactly those whose educational value was already weakest: generic, take-home, one-shot submissions that rewarded polish over thinking.
- A transparent classroom agreement about which AI uses are permitted for which tasks, combined with conversation rather than surveillance, produces better trust and better learning than a detection-oriented stance.
- Paying attention to why students misuse AI — overload, unclear expectations, lack of scaffolding — and addressing those conditions is often more effective at reducing misuse than trying to catch it after the fact.
