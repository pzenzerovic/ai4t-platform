## When AI Stops Giving Answers and Starts Teaching

One of the most remarkable capabilities of modern AI systems is their ability to function not just as information sources, but as interactive tutors. Instead of immediately delivering a finished answer, the AI can ask questions, guide the student through a reasoning process, adapt to their level of understanding, and provide feedback at each step.

This is fundamentally different from how most students initially use AI. As the previous lesson established, the default mode of interaction is transactional: ask a question, get an answer. But when AI is configured for tutoring, the interaction becomes a dialogue. The AI becomes a patient teacher who never gets frustrated, who can explain the same concept seventeen different ways, and who adjusts the difficulty in real time based on the student's responses.

This lesson explores how tutoring mode works in practice, how to activate it in major AI tools, and what its strengths and limitations are. Most importantly, it examines the teacher's role in making AI tutoring educationally effective rather than just technologically impressive.

## How to Activate Tutoring Mode

The critical thing to understand is that AI tools do not automatically enter a tutoring mode. By default, they respond to questions with direct answers. To shift the AI into a guided learning mode, the student (or teacher) must explicitly request it.

In ChatGPT, there is a dedicated *Study and Learn* mode that can be activated in the model settings before starting a conversation. When this mode is active, the model is prompted to ask questions rather than immediately provide solutions, break problems into steps, and encourage the student to articulate their own reasoning before receiving guidance.

In Claude, a similar effect can be achieved by selecting the *Learning* style in the response settings. This adjusts the model to focus on gradual knowledge building, guided explanation, and reflective questioning rather than direct answers.

However, the most reliable approach — and the one that works across all AI tools — is for the student to explicitly describe the learning mode they want. A prompt like "I am learning to solve linear equations. Do not give me the answer. Instead, guide me through the process step by step and ask me questions at each stage" will activate tutoring behaviour in any modern AI system, regardless of which specific tool is being used.

> **Key Insight**: The prompt engineering skills from Category 2 are directly relevant here. A student who has learned to write clear, specific prompts can activate tutoring mode in any AI tool — not just those with built-in learning features. The skill is transferable.

## Interactive Dialogue as the Foundation of Learning

When AI tutoring works well, it follows a pattern that experienced teachers will recognise immediately. The AI begins by assessing what the student already knows, typically through diagnostic questions like "What do you already know about this topic?" or "How would you start solving this problem?" Based on the student's response, the AI calibrates its approach — starting from basics if the student shows limited prior knowledge, or moving to more advanced aspects if the student demonstrates confidence.

This initial calibration serves a dual purpose. For the student, it activates prior knowledge, which educational research consistently identifies as one of the most important factors in effective learning. For the AI, it provides contextual information that shapes the quality and appropriateness of subsequent responses.

After the initial assessment, the AI guides the student through the material using questions rather than statements. Instead of saying "The answer is x = 4," it asks "What do you need to do to isolate the unknown? What is the next logical step?" This approach — often called the Socratic method — forces the student to do the thinking. The AI provides the scaffolding, but the cognitive effort belongs to the student.

## Adaptive Difficulty

One of the practical advantages of AI tutoring is its ability to adjust difficulty in real time. If a student shows uncertainty or makes repeated errors, the AI can simplify the task, offer additional examples, or break the problem into smaller steps. If the student demonstrates confidence and accuracy, the AI can increase complexity or pose more challenging questions.

This adaptive behaviour resembles the differentiation that teachers practise in the classroom, but it occurs at an individual level and in real time. In an ideal scenario, the student remains in what educational researchers call the zone of proximal development — the space where a task is challenging enough to promote growth but not so difficult that it causes frustration and disengagement.

For teachers, this means that AI tutoring can serve as a powerful supplement to classroom instruction. A student who needs additional practice or a different explanation can work with the AI tutor outside of class time, receiving personalised guidance that would be impossible for a single teacher to provide to every student simultaneously.

## A Practical Example: Mathematics

To make this concrete, consider a Year 6 student learning to solve linear equations. Instead of typing "Solve 2x + 3 = 11," the student writes: "I am learning to solve linear equations with one unknown. Guide me through solving this problem step by step and ask me questions — do not give me the answer immediately: 2x + 3 = 11."

The AI begins by checking the student's understanding: "What does it mean to solve an equation? Can you explain in your own words?" The student responds: "It means finding the value of x." The AI confirms and moves to the first step: "Good. Now look at the equation 2x + 3 = 11. The goal is to get x alone on one side. What is currently preventing that?" The student identifies the +3. The AI asks: "What should we do to both sides of the equation to remove the +3?" The student says "Subtract 3." The AI confirms and asks the student to perform the operation.

At each stage, the student is doing the thinking. The AI provides confirmation when the reasoning is correct and gentle redirection when it is not. When the student mistakenly suggests dividing by 8 instead of 2, the AI does not simply correct the error. It pauses and asks: "Think about this again. We have 2x = 8. The 2 is multiplying x. What operation undoes multiplication?"

This pattern — question, student response, feedback, next question — mirrors the best practices of one-on-one tutoring. Research consistently shows that individual tutoring is one of the most effective forms of instruction, and AI makes a version of it available to every student at any time.

> **Tip**: Teach students to request verification steps at the end of AI-guided problem solving. In the mathematics example above, the AI asked the student to substitute x = 4 back into the original equation to verify the answer. This habit of self-checking transfers directly to independent work.

## A Practical Example: Science

The tutoring approach is not limited to mathematics. Consider a Year 6 student learning about photosynthesis. The student writes: "Explain photosynthesis to me as a Year 6 student, but first ask me what I already know about the topic and guide me through understanding it."

The AI begins with a diagnostic question: "What do plants need to grow?" If the student mentions sunlight and water, the AI builds on that knowledge. If the student is uncertain, the AI starts with concrete, observable examples: "Think about the plants you see around you — trees, grass, flowers. People eat food to grow. Dogs eat food from a bowl. But what do plants eat?"

The AI then guides the student through a chain of reasoning — from the observation that plants do not consume food the way animals do, to the realisation that they use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce their own food. At each step, the AI asks the student to reason before providing information, building understanding through discovery rather than lecture.

## The Limits of the Digital Tutor

AI tutoring has significant limitations that teachers and students must understand clearly. The AI has no pedagogical intuition. It cannot read body language, detect frustration through tone of voice, or sense when a student is about to give up. It cannot assess the broader emotional context of a learning situation — whether a student is anxious about an upcoming test, struggling with problems at home, or simply having a bad day.

The AI also does not have a genuine understanding of what it is teaching. It produces responses that are statistically appropriate based on its training data, but it does not "know" mathematics or biology in the way a teacher does. This means it can occasionally provide incorrect guidance, especially in complex or nuanced situations. Students must be taught to verify AI-generated explanations against other sources, and teachers must periodically review the quality of AI tutoring interactions.

> **Warning**: AI tutoring should be understood as an additional layer of support, not a replacement for the teacher. The AI can handle routine guided practice and provide additional explanation outside of class time. But the meaning, structure, and direction of learning remain the teacher's responsibility. The AI is a tool; the teacher is the architect.

## The Teacher's Role

For AI tutoring to be educationally effective, the teacher must play several critical roles. First, the teacher must teach students how to activate and use tutoring mode — including how to write prompts that request guided learning rather than direct answers. This is a skill that needs to be explicitly taught and practised.

Second, the teacher must design the learning context. AI tutoring works best when students have a clear understanding of what they are trying to learn and why. The teacher provides this framework by setting learning objectives, connecting AI tutoring to classroom activities, and ensuring that students approach the AI with specific, well-defined learning goals.

Third, the teacher must monitor and evaluate. Periodically reviewing transcripts of student-AI tutoring sessions can reveal patterns — common misconceptions, recurring difficulties, or areas where the AI's guidance was inadequate. This information feeds back into classroom instruction, creating a cycle of improvement that neither the AI nor the student could achieve alone.

## Summary

- AI tools can function as interactive tutors that guide students through problems using questions and scaffolding rather than delivering direct answers, but this mode must be explicitly activated.
- Tutoring mode can be activated through built-in features (ChatGPT's Study and Learn mode, Claude's Learning style) or through explicit prompts that request guided learning — the latter approach works across all AI tools.
- Effective AI tutoring follows a pattern of diagnostic assessment, adaptive questioning, real-time difficulty adjustment, and step-by-step guidance that mirrors the best practices of individual human tutoring.
- The adaptive difficulty of AI tutoring allows it to keep students in the zone of proximal development, adjusting complexity based on demonstrated understanding.
- AI tutoring has significant limitations: it cannot read emotions, detect frustration, or understand the broader context of a student's learning situation, and it can occasionally provide incorrect guidance.
- The teacher's role shifts from being the sole source of guided instruction to being the designer of learning contexts, the trainer of student AI skills, and the evaluator of tutoring quality.
- AI tutoring is most effective as a supplement to classroom instruction — providing additional practice and personalised explanation — rather than as a standalone replacement for teacher-led learning.
